\chapter{INTRODUCTION}


\section{Background to the Study}

Student retention and academic success represent critical challenges confronting higher
 education institutions worldwide. According to \parencite{NCES2024}, undergraduate enrollment
  in degree-granting institutions decreased by 13\% between 2012 and 2022, while 23.3\% of
   undergraduate students leave universities annually. Six-year graduation rates reveal 
   substantial variation: 63\% at public universities, 68\% at private nonprofit institutions,
    and only 29\% at private for-profit institutions. This persistent attrition represents 
    significant personal and economic costs for individual students, alongside substantial 
    challenges for institutional sustainability and mission fulfillment.

Technology is changing that. Machine learning algorithms can now identify at-risk 
students weeks or even months before traditional warning signs appear, achieving 
prediction accuracies of 88–92\% \parencite{Ahmed2024, Gathani2026}. Georgia State
 University's GPS Advising system illustrates what becomes possible: by tracking 
 over 800 risk factors for 40,000 students daily and generating 90,000 targeted
  interventions each year, the university raised its graduation rate by 7.8 percentage
   points and virtually eliminated achievement gaps between demographic groups
    \parencite{GSU2024}. The technology, in short, is mature and proven—but
     its benefits have not yet reached most of the world.

Africa is a striking example of that gap. The African Union's Continental 
Education Strategy (CESA 2016–2025) acknowledges retention as a priority, 
yet most African universities still rely on end-of-semester transcripts as 
their primary diagnostic tool. Some sub-regions have begun to experiment. Mohammed V University in Morocco 
achieved an 81.66\% dropout-prediction accuracy in a pilot study that utilized L2-regularized logistic 
regression \parencite{Bouihi2024}. "Similarly, a study at the University of Johannesburg 
in South Africa utilized machine learning to predict the performance of 1,366 engineering students, 
achieving a 65.86\% accuracy rate using Decision Tree algorithms and identifying prior performance in
 physics and mathematics as primary success factors.  \parencite{Taodzera2016} 

Within East Africa, Kenya's higher education sector faces particularly acute retention pressures. 
Government capitation covers only 57\% of students against an 80\% target, while public universities
 have accumulated Ksh 62 billion in unpaid bills—structural constraints that make student attrition
  both more likely and more financially consequential for students, families, and institutions 
  \parencite{KIPPRA2024, DailyNation2024}. 
  
  Recognizing the challenge of student attrition, researchers 
  from the University of Nairobi in collaboration with one researcher at the Free University of Brussels conducted a study utilizing Machakos
   University College as a case study to apply Business Intelligence and machine learning to dropout
    prediction \parencite{Ngemu2015}. Using the WEKA toolkit to analyze historical institutional data, 
    the research team—comprising Ngemu, Odongo, Omulo, and Manderick—evaluated multiple classification 
    algorithms, including Naïve Bayes, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and the J48 decision tree.
     Their findings identified that at-risk students could be accurately flagged based on critical 
     predictors such as financial stability (fee payment), entry age, health status, and parental 
     occupation \parencite{Ngemu2015}.


Strathmore University operates within this challenging Kenyan landscape while maintaining distinct academic 
standards that intensify the consequences of delayed intervention. The institution enforces a 67\% minimum
 attendance threshold for examination eligibility and a 2.0 GPA floor for good academic standing—policy
  thresholds that render early identification especially critical, as students who breach them may be 
  barred from examinations or placed on academic probation regardless of their potential for recovery. 
  The university collects extensive student data through its Academic Management System (AMS), Learning 
  Management System (LMS), and attendance tracking platform, generating daily records on academic performance, 
  engagement behaviour, and class participation. 
  
  Despite this data richness, these systems operate as isolated 
  administrative repositories with no integrated analytics capability, preventing the transformation of raw
   institutional data into actionable early warnings. Students are typically identified for intervention only
    after manifesting lag indicators—cumulative grade decline, attendance threshold breach, or formal withdrawal
     notice—by which point the window for effective academic recovery has frequently closed. This reactive paradigm, 
     though common across Kenyan institutions, is inconsistent both with international best practice
      (Georgia State University, 2024) and with Kenya's national call for greater technology utilisation in higher
       education (Republic of Kenya Ministry of Education, 2024). This study addresses that gap directly: by developing 
       a machine learning-based predictive analytics platform calibrated to Strathmore's academic policies, data 
       infrastructure, and resource constraints, it seeks to transform the institution's support model from reactive
        monitoring to proactive, evidence-driven intervention.

\section{Statement of the Problem}

Strathmore University currently faces a critical gap between data collection and actionable 
student support. Although the institution maintains robust data streams through its Academic
 Management System (AMS), Learning Management System (LMS), and attendance tracking platforms, 
 these systems operate as isolated administrative repositories rather than integrated
  predictive tools. This fragmentation prevents the university from transforming raw 
  institutional data into early warnings for at-risk undergraduate students.

The primary consequence of this gap is a reactive support paradigm. Students are typically 
identified for intervention only after manifesting lag-indicators of failure, cumulative low 
grades, breach of the mandatory 67\% attendance threshold for examination eligibility, or 
formal withdrawal. By this stage, the window for effective academic recovery has often closed. 
This reactive approach is not only inefficient but inconsistent with the Republic of Kenya
 Ministry of Education's \parencite{MOE2024} Sessional Paper No. 1, which explicitly 
 identifies `inaccurate, unreliable data and inadequate utilisation of technology' as 
 a core bottleneck in the national education sector.



Despite possessing the necessary digital infrastructure and longitudinal student datasets, 
Strathmore University lacks a specialised machine learning architecture capable of integrating
 these data streams into a coherent, real-time risk stratification system. Without an 
 automated platform to predict dropout risk, course failure risk, and program delay 
 risk simultaneously, the university remains unable to allocate its limited counselling
  and mentoring resources to the students who need them most, and advisors are left to 
  act on intuition rather than evidence.

This study therefore develops, implements, and evaluates an integrated predictive analytics 
platform tailored to Strathmore University's academic policies, data infrastructure, 
and resource constraints. By producing explainable, actionable risk predictions across
 three distinct outcomes, the platform is designed to transition the institution from 
 reactive monitoring to proactive, data-driven intervention.
 
\section{Justification}
\subsection{Operational Necessity}
This research is justified by the need to bridge the gap between data collection and data 
utilization in Universities such as Strathmore University. Currently, Strathmore's student support is
reactive—interventions. It often occurs only after a student has already failed or dropped out.
 There is an urgent need for a system that transforms underutilized data into actionable early
 warnings without increasing the administrative burden on faculty.
\subsection{Addressing the "Black Box" Problem}
This study is justified by its focus on explainability. By using specialized techniques to show why a 
student is at risk, the research justifies the shift toward evidence-based mentoring, ensuring that 
 institutional support is credible.
\subsection{Policy and Economic Pressures}
This study responds to the Kenya Ministry of Education’s Sessional Paper No. 1 of 2024, which cites 
inadequate technology use as a core bottleneck in higher education \parencite{KIPPRA2024}. In a climate
 of rising enrollments and limited government funding, the status quo of universal mentoring is no 
 longer sustainable. This research is justified as an economic necessity to ensure that finite counseling 
 resources are directed only where they are most needed.

\section{Research Objectives}
\subsection{Main Objective}
To design, develop, and evaluate a machine learning-based predictive analytics platform for early identification of at-risk undergraduate students at Strathmore University.
\subsection{Specific Objectives}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item To consolidate and harmonise student data from institutional systems (AMS, LMS, and attendance tracking) into an integrated analytics pipeline capable of supporting real-time risk computation.
    \item To develop and validate explainable machine learning models that predict three distinct risk outcomes: dropout risk, course failure risk, and program delay risk.
    \item To design role-based dashboards for university administrators, school administrators, and academic mentors that present actionable risk predictions with supporting intervention guidance.
    \item To evaluate model accuracy and predictive performance using historical student cohort data (2021-2024) and standard machine learning evaluation metrics including Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC.
    \item To assess the platform's usability and perceived institutional impact through structured evaluation with academic advisors, school administrators, and mentors, with specific attention to intervention feasibility and long-term sustainability.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Questions}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item What student data features from AMS, LMS, and attendance systems are most predictive of dropout risk, course failure risk, and program delay risk?
    \item Which machine learning algorithms provide the most accurate and explainable predictions for each of the three risk categories, and what features drive each prediction?
    \item How can risk predictions be effectively presented to different stakeholder groups---university administrators, school administrators, and academic mentors---to support timely and informed decision-making?

\section{Scope and Delimitations of the Study}

\subsection{Scope}
This research focuses on the development and evaluation of a predictive analytics platform 
for approximately 5,000 undergraduate students across all five schools at Strathmore University,
 Kenya. The technical scope involves integrating data from the Academic Management System 
 (AMS), Learning Management System (LMS), and attendance tracking records into a unified 
 analytics pipeline. The study develops three prediction models—targeting dropout risk, 
 course failure risk, and program delay risk—using ensemble algorithms including Random 
 Forest, Gradient Boosting, and XGBoost, trained on historical student cohort data from 2021-2024 with known academic outcomes. Model predictions are accompanied by SHAP-based explainability outputs to ensure advisors and administrators can understand and act on the reasons behind each risk classification \parencite{Lundberg2017}. The resulting platform includes role-based dashboards for university administrators, school administrators, and academic mentors, with model performance measured through standard machine learning evaluation metrics (Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC) and platform effectiveness assessed through structured stakeholder feedback.

\subsection{Delimitations}
The research boundaries are defined by the following deliberate constraints. Regarding data factors, the analysis is restricted to academic risk factors measurable through institutional systems, such as attendance rates, GPA, LMS engagement, and course grades. External variables such as socioeconomic background, mental health status, and family circumstances are excluded, as they are not systematically captured in Strathmore's existing data infrastructure and their collection would raise ethical concerns beyond the current scope. In terms of student population, the study is limited to undergraduate students across Strathmore University's five schools; postgraduate and continuing education cohorts are excluded due to their unique assessment patterns. 

The system capabilities are delimited to identifying risk levels and suggesting general intervention categories, such as academic counselling or tutoring referrals, rather than providing course-specific remediation. Within the algorithmic scope, the study employs ensemble tree-based methods like Random Forest and XGBoost \parencite{Chen2016} rather than deep learning architectures. This decision is justified by the dataset size of approximately 5,000 students and the critical need for feature interpretability. Furthermore, the technical platform is developed as a web-based application, with mobile-native development remaining outside the scope. Financial domains, including fee payment risk and scholarship management, are also excluded to focus purely on academic and engagement indicators. Finally, the implementation phase concludes with a functional prototype and structured evaluation; full-scale production deployment and long-term longitudinal impact assessment are reserved for future research.

This study advances the field of Educational Data Mining (EDM) by operationalizing Tinto’s Student Integration Model within an
 African institutional context. It provides empirical evidence that Western retention theories can be mapped to quantifiable

 luxury but a prerequisite for AI adoption in resource-constrained environments, setting a new standard for how predictive 
 models should be evaluated beyond simple accuracy.

 It transforms the advisor’s role from a monitor to a facilitator, allowing for substantive, reason-led conversations

\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW}
\section{Introduction}
The purpose of this chapter is to synthesize the multidisciplinary body of research that underpins 
this study's design and motivation. The review spans three interconnected domains: the theoretical
 frameworks governing student retention, the global evolution of predictive analytics and Educational 
 Data Mining (EDM) in higher education, and the African and Kenyan institutional contexts in which this
  research is embedded. Together, these streams establish both the intellectual foundation and the practical 
  necessity of a machine learning-based Early Warning System (EWS) calibrated to Strathmore University's 
  academic policies and data infrastructure.

The chapter is organized to trace a deliberate logical arc. It begins with the macroeconomic and human costs
 of student attrition, then narrows progressively from global best practices to regional pilot studies, and 
 finally focuses on the specific institutional gaps this research is designed to fill. Central to this arc is
  the argument that modern machine learning does not merely add technological convenience—it operationalizes 
  classical retention theory, transforming abstract constructs such as academic integration and social engagement
   into measurable, actionable data signals.

  \section{Theoretical Frameworks for Student Retention}
  \subsection{Tinto's Student Integration Model}
  The dominant theoretical anchor for student retention research remains Vincent Tinto's Student Integration Model,
   which posits that a student's decision to persist or withdraw is governed by their degree of academic and social 
   integration into the institutional community (Tinto, 1975; 1987). Academic integration encompasses performance 
   relative to institutional standards, while social integration reflects the quality of peer and faculty relationships.
    When integration along either dimension is insufficient, the student's commitment to completing their degree erodes, 
    ultimately increasing the probability of departure.

This study operationalizes Tinto's model within a contemporary data environment by mapping its constructs onto
 quantifiable institutional indicators. Academic integration is proxied through variables captured in Strathmore's 
 Academic Management System (AMS)—specifically cumulative GPA, course grade trajectories, and proximity to the
  mandatory 2.0 GPA floor for good academic standing. Social and behavioral integration is approximated through
   engagement metrics derived from the Learning Management System (LMS), including login frequency, assignment 
   submission timeliness, and resource access patterns, as well as attendance data relative to the institution's 67\% threshold 
   for examination eligibility. By grounding the predictive models in Tinto's framework, this research ensures that technical 
   outputs retain theoretical interpretability, translating algorithmic risk scores into language that is credible to academic 
   advisors and institutional policymakers alike.

   \subsection{The Shift from Reactive to Proactive Intervention Paradigms}
   Across Kenyan higher education, the dominant support paradigm remains reactive: students are identified for
    intervention only after they have manifested what the literature terms 'lag indicators'—cumulative grade collapse, 
    attendance threshold breach, or formal withdrawal notice (Republic of Kenya Ministry of Education, 2024). By this 
    stage, the window for effective academic recovery has frequently closed, and the institutional response is necessarily 
    remedial rather than preventive.
An emergent body of scholarship advocates a fundamental reorientation toward proactive, evidence-driven intervention, 
in which behavioral and academic data are continuously analyzed to identify risk trajectories before they become academic 
crises (Ahmed, 2024; Gathani et al., 2026). This paradigm shift is not merely technical; it reflects a reconceptualization
 of the advisor's role—from a monitor who responds to failure to a facilitator who intervenes before failure occurs. 
 This study is explicitly positioned within this proactive paradigm, seeking to automate risk detection so that finite 
 counseling resources are directed toward students who demonstrate early warning signals rather than those who have 
 already crossed institutional thresholds.

\section{Machine Learning in Educational Data Mining (EDM)}
\subsection{The Paradigm Shift: From Lag Indicators to Predictive Signals}

Educational Data Mining (EDM) has undergone a profound methodological transformation over the past decade. 
Traditional approaches relied on 'lag indicators'—mid-semester grades, end-of-term transcripts, and formal
 withdrawal notices—which, by the time they are generated, often arrive too late for meaningful academic
  intervention (Ahmed, 2024). The emergence of machine learning has fundamentally altered this temporal dynamic.

Research by Ahmed (2024) and Gathani et al. (2026) demonstrates that modern ML algorithms can identify at-risk
 students with prediction accuracies ranging from 88\% to 92\%, often weeks or months before academic failure
  becomes irreversible. This temporal advance is the core value proposition of EWS architecture: it converts
   institutional data from a historical record into a forward-looking instrument.

   \subsection{Model Performance Metrics and Evaluation Standards}
   In the domain of student risk prediction, accuracy alone is an insufficient evaluation criterion.
    The asymmetric costs of misclassification—where failing to identify a genuinely at-risk student 
    carries far greater consequences than a false alarm—necessitate a more nuanced suite of evaluation metrics:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Recall (Sensitivity): The proportion of genuinely at-risk students correctly identified by the model. 
            High recall is the primary objective in EWS contexts, as missed detections represent failed interventions.
        \item Precision: The proportion of students flagged as at-risk who are genuinely at-risk. Precision governs the 
            credibility of the system; excessively low precision generates alert fatigue among advisors.
        \item F1-Score: The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, providing a single balanced metric that is particularly 
            informative when class distributions are imbalanced—a common characteristic of student datasets where at-risk students are a minority.
        \item ROC-AUC (Receiver Operating Characteristic – Area Under Curve): A threshold-independent measure of the model's 
            ability to discriminate between successful and at-risk students across all possible classification cutoffs. 
            A value approaching 1.0 indicates near-perfect discrimination.
    \end{itemize}

These metrics collectively ensure that model evaluation reflects real-world operational 
requirements rather than abstract statistical performance.

\subsection{ Ensemble Learning: The Technical Foundation}
To achieve the predictive stability required for institutional deployment, this research employs Ensemble 
Learning—a class of machine learning technique that combines the outputs of multiple algorithms to produce
 predictions that are more accurate and robust than any single model could achieve in isolation.

The three ensemble algorithms selected for this study each offer distinct advantages. Random Forest 
constructs multiple independent decision trees and aggregates their predictions through majority voting, 
reducing variance and mitigating overfitting—a particular risk when working with moderately sized institutional 
datasets of approximately 5,000 students (Breiman, 2001). Gradient Boosting builds trees sequentially, with each
 tree correcting the errors of its predecessor, producing a powerful predictor that is especially effective on 
 structured, tabular data characteristic of academic records. XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting), pioneered 
 by Chen and Guestrin (2016), extends the gradient boosting framework with regularization terms that prevent 
 overfitting, efficient handling of missing values, and computational optimization that enables scalable deployment. 
 Its proven performance on structured datasets and widespread adoption in applied predictive analytics make it 
 particularly well-suited to this research context.

The selection of ensemble tree-based methods over deep learning architectures is a deliberate methodological
 choice justified by two factors. First, the dataset size of approximately 5,000 undergraduate students is
  insufficient to fully exploit the representational capacity of deep neural networks without significant 
  risk of overfitting. Second, and critically, tree-based ensembles yield feature importance rankings that 
  are inherently more interpretable than deep learning's distributed representations, providing a natural 
  foundation for explainability tools.

  \subsection{The Black Box Problem and Explainable AI (XAI)}
  The adoption of complex ensemble models in institutional settings is frequently impeded by what the literature 
  terms the 'Black Box' problem: the inability of users—advisors, administrators, and students—to understand why a 
  particular prediction was made (Lundberg & Lee, 2017). In a university context, this opacity is not merely a 
  technical inconvenience. Advisors who cannot explain why a student has been flagged lack the conversational 
  foundation to initiate a credible, evidence-led intervention. Students who receive unexplained risk labels may
   feel stigmatized or surveilled rather than supported.

The most widely adopted framework for addressing this problem is SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), 
developed by Lundberg and Lee (2017) from cooperative game theory's Shapley values. SHAP assigns each feature 
a contribution value for each individual prediction, indicating both the direction and magnitude of that feature's 
influence on the model's output. For a student flagged with high dropout risk, SHAP might reveal that declining LMS 
engagement over the past three weeks is the primary driver, followed by attendance approaching the 67\% threshold and
 a drop in the most recent coursework grade.

In resource-constrained environments such as Kenyan universities, where institutional trust must be earned 
incrementally and advisor capacity is limited, explainability is not a luxury feature—it is a prerequisite for 
adoption. By transforming the advisor's interaction from 'this student is at risk' to 'this student is at risk 
because of these specific, documented behaviors,' SHAP-driven dashboards facilitate substantive, reason-led 
conversations that are both more effective and more defensible.

\subsection{Risk Outcome Categories in the Literature}
The existing EDM literature predominantly frames student risk as a binary problem—a student either persists or 
drops out. This study extends the literature by predicting three distinct risk outcomes simultaneously, reflecting
 the multi-dimensional nature of academic difficulty:

 \begin{itemize}
    \item Dropout Risk: The probability that a student will discontinue enrollment before completing their degree—the 
    most consequential outcome for both the student and the institution.
    \item Course Failure Risk: The likelihood of failing an individual course or becoming examination-ineligible due 
    to attendance falling below the 67\% threshold mandated by Strathmore University.
    \item Program Delay Risk: The probability that a student will exceed the standard programme duration due to 
    academic probation, course repetition, or accumulated failed semesters.
 \end{itemize}

By distinguishing these three outcomes, the platform enables differentiated intervention strategies rather than 
a single undifferentiated 'at-risk' label, allowing advisors to tailor responses to the specific nature of each 
student's vulnerability.

\section{Evidence of Technological Maturity}
The most extensively documented implementation of ML-based student success analytics is Georgia State University's 
GPS Advising system (Georgia State University, 2024). The system tracks over 800 risk factors daily for a student
 population of approximately 40,000, generating 90,000 targeted advisor interventions each academic year.
  The outcomes are remarkable in scale: the university achieved a 7.8 percentage point increase in its six-year
   graduation rate and virtually eliminated achievement gaps between demographic groups—outcomes that had previously
    been attributed to structural inequalities resistant to intervention.

The Georgia State case establishes two critical propositions for this research. First, it demonstrates that the 
technology is mature, proven, and deployable at institutional scale. Second, and more importantly, it illustrates 
the mechanism through which data-driven intervention closes equity gaps: by systematically identifying students who 
would otherwise be overlooked by advisor intuition, the system ensures that support is distributed on the basis of
 need rather than visibility. This principle is directly relevant to the Strathmore context, where advisor capacity 
 is finite and unevenly distributed across a population of approximately 5,000 undergraduate students.

 \section{Predictive Analytics in the African Higher Education Context}
 \subsection{Continental Policy Framework}
 The African Union's Continental Education Strategy for Africa (CESA 2016–2025) formally identifies student retention 
 as a continental priority, calling for systemic improvements in the quality and inclusivity of higher education 
 across member states. Despite this strategic commitment, the operational implementation of retention technology
  remains nascent across most of the continent. The dominant institutional practice continues to rely on end-of-semester 
  transcripts as the primary diagnostic tool—a paradigm that, by definition, identifies failure only after it 
  has occurred.

This gap between continental policy aspiration and institutional practice defines the landscape in which this
 study operates, providing both a mandate and an opportunity for evidence-based technology adoption.

 \subsection{Regional Case Studies}
 A small but growing body of African pilot studies demonstrates that ML can extract actionable risk signals 
 from institutional datasets, even under conditions of data noise and resource constraint.
In Morocco, Bouihi et al. (2024) conducted a dropout prediction study at Mohammed V University utilizing L2-regularized 
logistic regression—a technique that applies a mathematical penalty to prevent the model from overfitting sparse 
institutional data. The study achieved an accuracy rate of 81.66\%, demonstrating the efficacy of regularized models
 in the African institutional context and establishing a regional benchmark against which more sophisticated ensemble
  methods can be assessed.

In South Africa, Taodzera (2016) analyzed a cohort of 1,366 engineering students at the University of Johannesburg 
using Decision Tree algorithms, achieving an accuracy rate of 65.86\%. Despite this more modest performance, the study
 produced a theoretically significant finding: prior performance in physics and mathematics emerged as the primary
  predictors of engineering student success, suggesting that discipline-specific feature engineering is essential
   for contextually valid risk prediction. This insight informs the feature selection strategy of the current study.

These regional cases demonstrate that while African institutional datasets may present greater noise than their Western 
counterparts—due to data quality issues, inconsistent record-keeping, and structural gaps—machine learning can
 nonetheless extract patterns of sufficient fidelity to support targeted intervention.

 \section{The Kenyan Higher Education Landscape}
 \subsection{Structural and Economic Pressures}
 Within East Africa, Kenya's higher education sector operates under acute financial stress that directly amplifies 
 the consequences of student attrition. Government capitation currently covers only 57\% of students against an 80\% 
 target, while the accumulated unpaid obligations of public universities have reached Ksh 62 billion 
 (Daily Nation, 2024; KIPPRA, 2024). In this resource-constrained environment, each student who withdraws
  before completing their degree represents a compounded loss: for the student who exits with debt but without 
  a credential, and for the institution that absorbs the fixed costs of that student's enrollment without 
  recovering the full tuition investment.

This structural context transforms student retention from a pedagogical aspiration into an economic imperative.
The status quo of 'universal mentoring'—in which advisor attention is distributed broadly across the student 
population without systematic risk stratification—is demonstrably unsustainable in an environment of resource 
scarcity. Evidence-based allocation, directed by automated risk prediction, offers a more efficient and equitable 
alternative.

\subsection{National Policy Mandate}
The Republic of Kenya Ministry of Education's Sessional Paper No. 1 of 2024 on Reforming Education and Training 
for Sustainable Development explicitly identifies 'inaccurate, unreliable data and inadequate utilisation of
 technology' as a core bottleneck impeding the quality and efficiency of the national education system 
 (Republic of Kenya Ministry of Education, 2024). This policy declaration establishes a formal mandate for 
 institutional investment in data-driven student support systems, providing a regulatory context that supports
  the current research's implementation and replication agenda.

  \subsection{Foundational Kenyan Study: Machakos University College}
  A foundational study in the Kenyan EDM literature was conducted by Ngemu et al. (2015) at Machakos University College, 
  utilizing the WEKA machine learning toolkit to evaluate multiple classification algorithms on historical institutional 
  data. The study assessed Naïve Bayes, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and the J48 Decision Tree algorithm, 
  identifying four variables as the primary predictors of student dropout risk:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Financial stability, specifically measured through fee payment status and timeliness.
    \item Entry age, reflecting the relationship between non-traditional enrollment age and retention challenges.
    \item Health status, capturing the impact of chronic illness or disability on academic continuity.
    \item Parental occupation, serving as a proxy for socioeconomic background and domestic support structures.
  \end{itemize}

While the Machakos study demonstrates that even basic machine learning tools applied to existing institutional 
records can yield actionable risk signals, it also highlights important limitations relative to the current research.
 The study relied on a limited set of variables predominantly capturing socioeconomic background rather than 
 real-time behavioral engagement, and it employed single-algorithm approaches rather than the ensemble methods 
 that have since been established as superior in terms of predictive stability. This study extends the Kenyan 
 literature by incorporating LMS engagement metrics as behavioral lead indicators, deploying ensemble learning
 for enhanced accuracy, and producing SHAP-based explanations that are absent from prior Kenyan research.

 \section{Data Integration and Institutional System Architecture}
 \subsection{Siloed Administrative Repositories}
 A persistent structural challenge in the deployment of institutional EWS is the fragmentation of student data
  across isolated administrative systems. In most universities, including Strathmore, student information is 
  distributed across at least three distinct platforms: the Academic Management System (AMS), which maintains 
  official records of enrollment, academic history, course grades, and demographic information; the Learning
   Management System (LMS), which captures behavioral engagement data such as login frequency, resource access,
    and assignment submission patterns; and attendance tracking infrastructure, which records real-time class 
    participation.

These systems were designed as independent administrative tools rather than integrated analytics 
infrastructure, and they typically lack the interoperability required for unified risk computation. 
The consequence is a 'fragmentation gap': institutions possess the raw data necessary for sophisticated 
risk prediction but lack the pipeline architecture to harmonize and analyze it in real time (Republic 
of Kenya Ministry of Education, 2024).

\subsection{Engagement Metrics as Lead Indicators}
A critical contribution of the EDM literature has been the identification of LMS engagement metrics as 'lead 
indicators'—behavioral signals that precede academic decline by days or weeks and therefore provide an earlier
 intervention window than grade-based measures alone. Research consistently demonstrates that declining login 
 frequency, missed assignment deadlines, and reduced resource access correlate strongly with subsequent academic 
 failure, often manifesting two to four weeks before a course grade reflects the underlying disengagement
  (Ahmed, 2024; Gathani et al., 2026).

For Strathmore University, where attendance records are maintained in real time and LMS activity is comprehensively 
logged, these engagement metrics represent an underutilized predictive resource. By integrating them with AMS
 academic records through a unified analytics pipeline, this study unlocks their lead-indicator value for the 
 first time within the institution's data environment.

 \section{Summary and Identification of Knowledge Gaps}
 This literature review has traced the intellectual lineage and operational context of this study across four
  interconnected domains. Theoretically, Tinto's Student Integration Model provides the conceptual architecture 
  through which student risk is understood: as a function of diminishing academic and social integration that 
  can be detected through behavioral and performance data before withdrawal occurs. Technologically, the EDM 
  literature demonstrates that ensemble machine learning algorithms—particularly Random Forest, Gradient Boosting,
   and XGBoost—can achieve prediction accuracies of 88–92\% on institutional datasets, and that SHAP-based 
   explainability transforms opaque risk scores into advisor-actionable intelligence. Regionally, studies in
    Morocco, South Africa, and Kenya confirm that ML can extract meaningful risk signals from African institutional 
    data, though existing studies are constrained by limited algorithm sophistication, narrow variable sets, 
    and the absence of real-time integration. Institutionally, Strathmore University possesses the data 
    infrastructure necessary for a sophisticated EWS but currently operates those systems as isolated 
    administrative silos within a reactive support paradigm.

This review identifies a specific and critical gap in the existing literature: no prior study has developed an
 integrated, real-time risk stratification system that simultaneously satisfies four conditions: (1) harmonizing 
 multiple institutional data streams—AMS, LMS, and attendance records—into a unified analytics pipeline; (2)
  predicting three distinct and clinically relevant risk outcomes (dropout risk, course failure risk, and program 
  delay risk) rather than a single binary outcome; (3) calibrating predictive models to the specific academic
   policy thresholds of a Kenyan institution, including the 67\% attendance requirement and the 2.0 GPA floor 
that characterize Strathmore's academic standards; and (4) producing SHAP-driven, role-differentiated
explanations that enable non-technical academic advisors to conduct evidence-led, credible interventions.

This study is designed to fill precisely that gap. Chapter Three presents the research methodology through which
these four objectives are operationalized, detailing the data pipeline architecture, feature engineering strategy,
model development and validation approach, and the design of the role-based dashboard interface.
