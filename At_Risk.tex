\section{Background to the Study}

Student retention is a persistent structural challenge in higher education
worldwide. Undergraduate enrolment in U.S.\ degree-granting institutions declined
by 13\% between 2012 and 2022, with 23.3\% of students leaving annually;
six-year graduation rates stand at 68\% for private nonprofits, 63\% for public
universities, and only 29\% for for-profit institutions
\parencite{irwin2024}. A systematic review of 125 peer-reviewed studies concluded
that, despite decades of institutional intervention, dropout remains a globally
significant and structurally unresolved problem \parencite{Goncalves2024}.

Two complementary frameworks explain the mechanisms driving this attrition.
Tinto's Student Integration Model \parencite{Tinto1975} holds that persistence
depends on a student's ongoing academic and social integration---framing dropout
as a longitudinal process in which declining GPA and reduced engagement signal
deteriorating belonging rather than isolated academic failure. Astin's
Input-Environment-Output (I-E-O) Model \parencite{Astin1993} provides the variable
architecture, categorising student inputs, institutional environmental
conditions, and measurable outcomes, and confirming that factors such as advisor
interaction, LMS engagement, and attendance are independently associated with
retention \parencite{Xiao2020}. Together, these frameworks define the feature
selection and outcome structure of this study.

Machine learning has transformed how institutions can respond to these dynamics.
Predictive algorithms can now identify at-risk students weeks before conventional
warning signs appear, achieving accuracies of 88--92\%
\parencite{ahmed2024, gathani2026}. A systematic review of 72 Educational Data
Mining (EDM) studies confirmed that ensemble methods---particularly XGBoost and
Random Forest---consistently outperform classical classifiers, while SHAP-based
explainability has emerged as a critical prerequisite for institutional adoption
\parencite{aldowah2026, kwakye2025}. Georgia State University's GPS Advising system
exemplifies the institutional impact achievable: tracking over 800 risk factors
for 40{,}000 students daily raised its graduation rate by 7.8 percentage points
and eliminated achievement gaps across demographic groups \parencite{gsu2024}.

These benefits have yet to reach most African institutions. Mohammed~V University
in Morocco achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.993 using an open-source XGBoost--SHAP
pipeline \parencite{bettahi2025}, and Wollo University in Ethiopia demonstrated that
ensemble classifiers reach 96\% accuracy on large-scale African institutional
data \parencite{esmael2024}---yet both studies, alongside South African work at the
University of Johannesburg \parencite{taodzera2016} and the University of Cape Town
\parencite{hassen2020}, share a consistent gap: the absence of student-level SHAP
explainability that would make predictions actionable for advisors. In Kenya
specifically, the earliest published study---at Machakos University
College---identified fee payment difficulty as the dominant attrition predictor,
positioning financial exclusion as the primary structural driver of dropout in
Kenyan higher education \parencite{ngemu2015}. Most African universities, however,
still rely on end-of-semester transcripts as their primary diagnostic tool.

Strathmore University operates within this landscape while enforcing a 67\%
minimum attendance threshold for examination eligibility and a 2.0~GPA floor for
academic standing---thresholds that make late identification especially costly.
The institution generates daily student data through its Academic Management
System~(AMS), Learning Management System~(LMS), and attendance tracking
platform, yet these systems operate as isolated repositories with no integrated
analytics capability. Students are identified for intervention only after
breaching these thresholds, by which point the window for effective recovery has
often closed. This study addresses that gap by developing a machine
learning-based predictive analytics platform---integrating all three data
streams, predicting dropout, course failure, and programme delay simultaneously,
and embedding SHAP explainability throughout---to transition Strathmore from
reactive monitoring to proactive, evidence-driven student support.

\section{Statement of the Problem}

Strathmore University possesses the digital infrastructure necessary for
proactive student support---an Academic Management System~(AMS), a Learning
Management System~(LMS), and an attendance tracking platform---yet these systems
operate as isolated administrative repositories with no integrated analytics
capability. As a result, the institution cannot transform its own institutional
data into early warnings for at-risk students. Students are identified for
intervention only after breaching the mandatory 67\% attendance threshold for
examination eligibility or the 2.0~GPA floor for academic standing---by which
point the window for effective recovery has often closed. The outcomes that
follow are both predictable and preventable: students drop out of programmes they
could have completed, fail courses that timely referrals could have salvaged, and
accumulate delays that extend their studies by entire semesters---each
representing a personal and institutional loss that earlier detection would have
averted \parencite{bettahi2025, esmael2024, gsu2024}.

Three interconnected capability gaps sustain this reactive paradigm. First,
existing systems cannot detect deteriorating engagement trajectories---declining
LMS login frequency, late assignment submissions, and falling attendance---weeks
before they manifest as failed grades or breached thresholds, despite research
confirming that such time-based signals carry greater predictive power than
static GPA values alone \parencite{dirin2022, morris2005}. Second, no
student-level explainability exists: advisors cannot identify which specific
factors are driving a student's deterioration and therefore cannot conduct
evidence-based intervention conversations---a well-documented barrier to
institutional adoption of predictive analytics \parencite{kwakye2025, zhang2021}.
Third, the binary persist-or-dropout framing of prior tools fails to capture the
full spectrum of academic difficulty; course failure risk and programme delay
risk are distinct, actionable outcomes that demand simultaneous, policy-calibrated
prediction alongside dropout risk \parencite{bettahi2025}.

This study addresses these gaps by developing, implementing, and evaluating an
integrated machine learning-based predictive analytics tool tailored to
Strathmore University's academic policies, data infrastructure, and resource
constraints. By unifying the AMS, LMS, and attendance data streams into a single
risk stratification pipeline, training open-source ensemble models on the
2021--2024 historical student cohort, and embedding SHAP-based explainability
throughout, the platform simultaneously predicts dropout risk, course failure
risk, and programme delay risk---each calibrated to the institution's 67\%
attendance threshold and 2.0~GPA floor. The goal is to transition Strathmore
from a reactive support model, in which advisors act on crisis, to a proactive
one, in which limited counselling resources are directed by evidence to the
students who need them most.

\section{Research Objectives}
\subsection{Main Objective}
To design, develop, and evaluate a machine learning-based predictive analytics tool for early identification of at-risk undergraduate students at Strathmore University.

\subsection{Specific Objectives}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item To design a Machine Learning predictive tool for university administrators, school administrators, and academic mentors that present actionable risk predictions with supporting intervention guidance.
    \item To evaluate model accuracy and predictive performance using historical student cohort data (2021--2024) and standard machine learning evaluation metrics including Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC.
    \item To assess the platform's usability and perceived institutional impact through structured evaluation with academic advisors, school administrators, and mentors, with specific attention to intervention feasibility and long-term sustainability.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Questions}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How can risk predictions be effectively presented to different stakeholder groups---university administrators, school administrators, and academic mentors---to support informed decision-making?
    \item To what extent does training predictive models on historical student cohort data (2021--2024) with known outcomes produce valid and generalisable predictions for current enrolled students?
    \item How do academic advisors, school administrators, and mentors perceive the platform's usability, intervention feasibility, and potential for long-term institutional adoption?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Justification}

\subsection{Operational Necessity}
This research is justified by the urgent need to bridge the gap between data
collection and data utilisation at Strathmore University. Currently, the
institution's student support is reactive---interventions occur only after a
student has already failed a course, breached the 67\% attendance threshold, or
submitted a withdrawal notice. The EDM literature is unambiguous that this
reactive paradigm is both preventable and institutionally costly: Georgia State
University's GPS Advising system demonstrated that proactive, data-driven
intervention can raise graduation rates by 7.8 percentage points and generate
over 90{,}000 annual adviser-student contacts \parencite{gsu2024}. Strathmore
possesses the necessary digital infrastructure---AMS, LMS, and attendance
tracking---but lacks the integrated ML architecture to operationalise it. There
is therefore an urgent need for a system that transforms underutilised data into
actionable early warnings without increasing the administrative burden on faculty.

\subsection{Addressing the Explainability Gap}
This study is additionally justified by its focus on explainability as a design
prerequisite rather than an optional enhancement. A systematic analysis of
existing African EDM studies reveals a consistent pattern: high-performing models
at Machakos University College, Wollo University, the University of Johannesburg,
and the University of Cape Town all lack SHAP-based or equivalent explainability
layers, which prevents their outputs from being translated into student-specific
intervention recommendations \parencite{esmael2024, hassen2020, ngemu2015,
taodzera2016}. The practical consequence is that advisors cannot determine which
specific factors triggered a risk classification for an individual student---
undermining the evidence-based advising that proactive support requires. By
embedding SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) throughout the platform's
prediction pipeline, this study operationalises the finding that explainable
predictions provide intuitive decision support and guidance for education
administrators \parencite{zhang2021}, and demonstrates that SHAP-based
explainability is not a luxury but a prerequisite for AI adoption in
resource-constrained environments \parencite{kwakye2025}.

\subsection{Policy and Economic Pressures}
This study responds directly to the Kenya Ministry of Education's Sessional
Paper No.~1 of 2024, which cites inadequate technology use as a core bottleneck
in higher education \parencite{kippra2024}. In a climate of rising enrolments and
limited government funding---where capitation covers only 57\% of students and
public universities have accumulated Ksh~62 billion in unpaid bills
\parencite{dailynation2024, kippra2024}---the status quo of universal,
intuition-based mentoring is no longer sustainable. The \textcite{bettahi2025}
open-source pipeline at Mohammed~V University establishes that a
high-performance, explainable Early Warning System can be deployed entirely using
open-source tools (Python, scikit-learn, XGBoost, SHAP) without commercial
licensing costs---directly resolving the affordability constraint that renders the
Georgia State GPS model non-transferable to the African context.
This research is therefore justified as both an economic necessity---ensuring that
finite counselling resources are directed to where they are most needed---and as a
replicable policy instrument for comparable East African institutions.

\section{Scope and Delimitations of the Study}

\subsection{Scope}
This research focuses on the development and evaluation of a predictive analytics platform for approximately 5,000 undergraduate students across all five schools at Strathmore University, Kenya. The technical scope involves integrating data from the Academic Management System (AMS), Learning Management System (LMS), and attendance tracking records into a unified analytics pipeline. The study develops three prediction models---targeting dropout risk, course failure risk, and program delay risk---using ensemble algorithms including Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and XGBoost, trained on historical student cohort data from 2021--2024 with known academic outcomes. Model predictions are accompanied by SHAP-based explainability outputs to ensure advisors and administrators can understand and act on the reasons behind each risk classification \parencite{Lundberg2017}. The resulting platform includes role-based dashboards for university administrators, school administrators, and academic mentors, with model performance measured through standard machine learning evaluation metrics (Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC) and platform effectiveness assessed through structured stakeholder feedback.

\subsection{Delimitations}
The research boundaries are defined by the following deliberate constraints. Regarding data factors, the analysis is restricted to academic risk factors measurable through institutional systems, such as attendance rates, GPA, LMS engagement, and course grades. External variables such as socioeconomic background, mental health status, and family circumstances are excluded, as they are not systematically captured in Strathmore's existing data infrastructure and their collection would raise ethical concerns beyond the current scope. In terms of student population, the study is limited to undergraduate students across Strathmore University's five schools; postgraduate and continuing education cohorts are excluded due to their unique assessment patterns.

The system capabilities are delimited to identifying risk levels and suggesting general intervention categories, such as academic counselling or tutoring referrals, rather than providing course-specific remediation. Within the algorithmic scope, the study employs ensemble tree-based methods like Random Forest and XGBoost \parencite{Chen2016} rather than deep learning architectures. This decision is justified by the dataset size of approximately 5,000 students and the critical need for feature interpretability. Furthermore, the technical platform is developed as a web-based application, with mobile-native development remaining outside the scope. Financial domains, including fee payment risk and scholarship management, are also excluded to focus purely on academic and engagement indicators. Finally, the implementation phase concludes with a functional prototype and structured evaluation; full-scale production deployment and long-term longitudinal impact assessment are reserved for future research.

\section{Significance}

\subsection{Theoretical and Methodological Significance}
This study advances Educational Data Mining (EDM) by operationalising Tinto's

and attendance breach flags calibrated to Strathmore's 67\% threshold, and

\subsection{Practical Significance}
For \textit{institutions}, the study delivers an open-source blueprint for

\subsection{National and Policy Significance}
At the national level, this research provides a replicable model for Kenya's

\section{Theoretical Frameworks for Student Retention}

This section is anchored in two complementary theoretical frameworks that
together provide the conceptual architecture for understanding and predicting
student attrition. Tinto's Student Integration Model explains the longitudinal
mechanism through which diminishing academic and social integration drives
departure, while Astin's I-E-O Model provides the variable
architecture --- categorizing student inputs, institutional environmental
conditions, and measurable outcomes --- that a predictive system must capture.
Both frameworks were developed in Western institutional contexts but carry
constructs directly mappable to Strathmore University's data infrastructure.

\subsection{Tinto's Student Integration Model}

The dominant theoretical anchor for student retention research remains Vincent
Tinto's Student Integration Model (SIM). First, it was proposed by Tinto and
Cullen in 1973 and formally published as a peer-reviewed synthesis in 1975. This
model draws its conceptual foundations from \'{E}mile Durkheim's theory of suicide---
specifically the proposition that a deficit of social cohesion precipitates
withdrawal from the community \parencite{Tinto1975}. Tinto's scholarly influence
has been exceptional: his works have been cited in more than 700 studies across
multiple disciplines, establishing the SIM as the most widely adopted
theoretical lens in higher education retention research \parencite{Bento2025}.

At its core, the SIM conceptualizes the university as a microcosm comprising two
interdependent systems---the academic and the social---and posits that a student's
persistence is governed by their degree of integration into both
\parencite{Tinto1975, Tinto1993}. Critically, Tinto frames dropout not as a
single discrete event but as a \textit{longitudinal process}: the student's
commitment to persist is continuously reconfigured through ongoing interactions.
This interactional character includes the cumulative weight of how individual
attributes, academic performance, and social relationships interact and
accumulate over time \parencite{Tinto1993, Nicoletti2019}.

\subsubsection{The Longitudinal Mechanism}

The SIM operates through a structured, multi-stage sequence linking
pre-institutional characteristics to eventual persistence:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/TintoModel1.jpg}
    \caption{Tinto's Model of Institutional Departure}
    \label{fig:tinto_model}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pre-Entry Attributes:} Students enter the institution carrying
    three foundational background variables: family background, individual skills
    and abilities, and prior schooling. These attributes do not directly determine
    persistence but collectively shape the intellectual and social disposition the
    student brings to the institution \parencite{Tinto1975}.

    \item \textbf{Initial Goals and Commitments:} Pre-entry attributes feed into
    two parallel commitment structures. \textit{Intentions and goal commitments}
    reflect the student's desire to earn a degree, while \textit{goal and
    institutional commitments} reflect the desire to graduate from a specific
    institution. Alongside these sits a third force---\textit{external
    commitments}---representing obligations outside the institution, such as
    family or employment, which compete with academic integration from the outset
    \parencite{Tinto1975, Tinto1993}.

    \item \textbf{Institutional Experiences:} Once enrolled, students interact
    simultaneously with two systems. The \textit{Academic System} operates
    through both formal channels---academic performance---and informal
    channels---faculty and staff interactions. The \textit{Social System}
    operates through formal extracurricular activities and informal peer group
    interactions. Both systems are embedded within and influenced by the broader
    \textit{External Community} \parencite{Tinto1993}.

    \item \textbf{Integration:} The quality of institutional experiences
    determines the degree of integration achieved along two dimensions.
    \textit{Academic integration} results from engagement with the Academic
    System, while \textit{social integration} results from engagement with the
    Social System. These two dimensions are interdependent---weakness in either
    increases the probability of departure \parencite{Tinto1975, Nicoletti2019}.

    \item \textbf{Re-evaluated Goals and Commitments:} Integration feeds back
    into a revised set of commitments---updated intentions, goal and institutional
    commitments, and external commitments---which are now reconfigured by the
    student's actual lived experience within the institution. This re-evaluation
    stage is what makes the model longitudinal and iterative rather than static:
    commitments are not fixed at entry but continuously reshaped over time
    \parencite{Nicoletti2019, Tinto1993}.

    \item \textbf{Departure Decision (Outcome):} The re-evaluated commitments
    produce the final outcome: a \textit{departure decision}. If the cumulative
    weight of academic and social integration has reinforced the student's goal
    and institutional commitment, persistence follows. If integration has been
    insufficient and external commitments or personal costs outweigh the perceived
    benefits of continued enrollment, departure follows. Critically, the entire
    process unfolds across time (\textit{t}), underscoring that dropout is a
    process, not a moment \parencite{Tinto1975}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{The Two Pillars of Integration}

Tinto distinguishes two primary and interdependent dimensions of integration,
both of which must be achieved to sustain persistence \parencite{Tinto1993}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Academic Integration:} encompasses both structural and normative
    dimensions. Structural integration is measured by grade performance (GPA)
    relative to institutional standards, while normative integration reflects
    intellectual development and alignment with the academic community's values.
    Grades function not merely as performance records but as institutional signals
    of belonging within the academic system; a declining GPA is therefore not
    simply a performance metric but a symptom of deteriorating academic
    integration \parencite{Tinto1975, Bento2025}.

    \item \textbf{Social Integration:} reflects the quality of peer-group
    interactions and faculty relationships. Research on blended and online
    learning environments has confirmed that when instructors provided timely
    support, appropriate feedback, and interactive engagement, students were
    substantially more likely to persist \parencite{Ivankova2007}. Furthermore,
    studies measuring LMS engagement have established that completers consistently
    demonstrate significantly higher levels of participation in learning
    activities---measured through discussion posts, resource access, and login
    frequency---than withdrawers, validating these behavioral signals as digital
    proxies for integration \parencite{Morris2005}.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Limitation Critical Assessment}

Despite its canonical status, the SIM carries limitations directly relevant
to this study. Its key constructs---integration, commitment, and fit---lack
precise operational definitions, creating interpretive challenges when
translating the model into computable variables \parencite{Nicoletti2019}.
The model was also developed within a Western, residential university context
and requires careful recalibration before application at Strathmore University.

More fundamentally, traditional SIM-based research relies on retrospective,
correlational designs: the model identifies what predicts departure but does
not generate prospective, real-time predictions for individual students. This
retrospective limitation is the precise gap that machine learning addresses---
where the SIM identifies the structural risk factors, ML determines when those
factors are currently activating for a specific student.

\subsubsection{Operationalization in This Study}

This study operationalizes Tinto's model within Strathmore's digital
infrastructure by systematically mapping each theoretical construct onto a
measurable institutional variable. The mapping is deliberate and bidirectional:
every predictor variable selected for the ML models corresponds to a specific
SIM construct, ensuring that algorithmic outputs retain theoretical
interpretability.

\subsection{Astin's Input-Environment-Output (I-E-O) Model}

Alongside Tinto's SIM, Alexander Astin's Input-Environment-Output (I-E-O)
model provides a complementary and practically powerful theoretical lens for
this study. Originally developed as a conceptual guide for institutional
assessment, the model was empirically consolidated in Astin's landmark study
\textit{What Matters in College? Four Critical Years Revisited}, which drew on
surveys of more than 20,000 students, 25,000 faculty members, and 200
institutions to examine how academic programmes, faculty, peer groups, and
institutional policies shape student development and outcomes
\parencite{Astin1993}. The model's philosophical foundation is a
\textit{talent development} approach to institutional excellence: quality is
not measured by institutional prestige or resource endowment, but by the
degree to which the institution succeeds in developing the potential of the
students it admits \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. This orientation makes the
I-E-O model especially relevant to the Strathmore context, where the goal is
not to rank students but to identify and support those whose potential is at
risk of being lost.

\subsubsection{Structure of the Model}

The I-E-O model organizes the variables relevant to student success into three
sequential, interacting components \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/AstinModel.png}
    \caption{Astin's Input-Environment-Output (I-E-O) Model (1993)}
    \label{fig:ieo_model}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inputs} refer to the personal qualities and characteristics
    students bring with them at the point of entry. This includes demographic
    attributes (age, sex), family background, prior academic achievement such
    as secondary school GPA, and initial motivations and goals. Astin's
    framework treats no single input as deterministic; rather, inputs establish
    the baseline against which the effects of institutional experiences must
    be measured \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.

    \item \textbf{Environment} encompasses the full range of institutional
    experiences, programmes, policies, and interactions to which the student
    is exposed during enrollment. This includes both tangible elements
    (classrooms, library resources, residential halls) and intangible
    elements (teaching pedagogy, administrative policies, peer group
    composition, and the quality of faculty interaction). Environmental
    variables are particularly significant in the I-E-O framework because
    they represent the factors that educators and institutional administrators
    can directly control to maximize talent development
    \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. Applied research using this framework has
    confirmed that student-advisor interaction, student-faculty interaction,
    extracurricular participation, and library utilization are each positively
    and independently associated with first-to-second year retention
    \parencite{Xiao2020}.

    \item \textbf{Outputs} (or Outcomes) represent the measurable results of
    the educational programme: academic achievement, retention, degree
    completion, and the full range of skills, values, and attitudes the
    institution aims to develop. In the I-E-O framework, outcomes are
    understood as the product of how environmental conditions transform a
    student's initial input characteristics. Evaluating outcomes without
    accounting for inputs is explicitly identified by Astin as an incomplete
    assessment design, as it fails to capture how much students actually
    developed relative to their starting point \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Methodological Logic: The Natural Experiment}

A defining methodological feature of the I-E-O model is its use of what
Astin terms \textit{natural experiments}. Because students cannot be randomly
assigned to different institutional environments, the model relies on complex
multivariate statistical analyses---such as multiple logistic regression---to
control for input differences and isolate the true effect of environmental
variables on outcomes \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. This approach offers two
practical advantages. First, it avoids the artificial conditions of laboratory
experiments by studying real institutional variation. Second, it allows
researchers to examine the simultaneous effects of many environmental
variables, which is precisely the analytical challenge confronted in this
study's multi-stream data pipeline.

\subsubsection{Complementarity with Tinto's Model}

The I-E-O model and Tinto's SIM are theoretically complementary rather than
competing. Tinto explains \textit{why} students depart through the mechanism
of diminishing integration; Astin provides the \textit{variable architecture}---
the structured categorization of inputs, environmental conditions, and
outcomes---that a predictive model must measure \parencite{Nicoletti2019,
Bento2025}. This complementarity is particularly significant for the
three-outcome prediction framework of this study. Dropout risk maps most
directly to Tinto's integration failure mechanism. Course failure risk and
programme delay risk, however, are better understood through Astin's
environmental lens: they reflect whether the institution's academic policies,
attendance requirements, and support resources are functioning as effective
environmental buffers against disengagement.

\subsubsection{Operationalization in this Study}

The I-E-O model maps onto the three institutional data streams in this
study's analytics pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inputs} are operationalized through Academic Management System (AMS)
    variables: entry qualifications, prior GPA trajectory, school of enrollment,
    and year of study---establishing baseline characteristics against which
    environmental effects are assessed \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.

    \item \textbf{Environment} is operationalized through LMS engagement metrics and
    attendance records. Strathmore's 67\% attendance threshold and 2.0 GPA floor
    function as measurable environmental constraints explicitly reflected in the
    predictive models. Prior research confirms that engagement variables are
    positively associated with retention \parencite{Xiao2020}.

    \item \textbf{Outputs} are the three predicted risk outcomes: dropout, course
    failure, and programme delay---extending Astin's framework beyond the
    graduation/dropout binary to capture the fuller spectrum of academic
    difficulty \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Limitation Critical Assessment}

The I-E-O model carries documented limitations relevant to this study.
Developed within a Western, residential, four-year university context, it
assumes full-time on-campus participation as the primary environmental channel
\parencite{Astin1993}---an assumption requiring recalibration for commuter,
part-time, or non-Western settings \parencite{Karp2008}.

The model's linear input-to-environment-to-output structure further
oversimplifies student development. In practice, outputs such as academic
performance continuously reshape subsequent engagement, creating feedback
loops a static framework cannot capture \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. These
limitations do not invalidate the I-E-O model as a theoretical anchor; rather,
they motivate a longitudinal machine learning architecture that continuously
re-evaluates risk as new engagement and performance data arrives---compensating
for the model's structural simplifications in live institutional deployment.

\section{Machine Learning in Educational Data Mining (EDM)}

\subsection{Overview of EDM}

Educational Data Mining (EDM) is a rapidly developing interdisciplinary
field that investigates techniques for identifying significant patterns in
large-scale educational datasets to enhance teaching, learning, and
institutional efficacy \parencite{Bello2025}. Within this domain, Machine
Learning (ML) has become a foundational tool, shifting EDM from theoretical
investigation into a practical instrument for data-driven decision-making.
By applying computational analysis to data originating in educational
contexts---such as student information systems, Learning Management Systems
(LMS), and behavioral interaction logs---ML algorithms identify hidden
patterns to forecast future student performance and behavior
\parencite{Dirin2022}. A recent systematic review of 72 peer-reviewed
studies concluded that research in this domain has matured significantly,
with ensemble methods, deep learning, and explainable AI now representing
the three dominant technical paradigms \parencite{Aldowah2026}.

\subsection{Core Applications of Machine Learning in Higher Education}

The primary applications of machine learning in EDM converge on four
objectives that are directly relevant to this study:

\subsubsection{Performance Prediction}
Academic performance prediction involves forecasting outcomes---such as
final course grades, semester GPA, or examination results---by analyzing
historical academic records, engagement trajectories, and behavioral
patterns. These models allow institutions to move beyond end-of-term
assessment toward continuous, data-informed monitoring of student progress
\parencite{Kwakye2025}.

\subsubsection{Risk Identification}
Early risk identification applies ML models to flag at-risk students early
in a term, enabling timely and targeted interventions before failure becomes
irreversible. This is the primary objective of the Early Warning System
(EWS) architecture developed in this study \parencite{Bello2025}.

\subsubsection{Study Path and Programme Guidance}
Programme guidance uses algorithmic predictions of student preferences,
strengths, and motivational orientations to recommend appropriate academic
specializations. Research at Haaga-Helia University demonstrated that
Random Forest (94\% accuracy) and Decision Tree (93\% accuracy) are
effective classifiers for study path selection, outperforming logistic
regression when non-linear motivational and demographic features are
included \parencite{Dirin2022}.

\subsubsection{Adaptive and Personalized Learning}
Personalized learning supports the creation of intelligent environments
that tailor content, pacing, and feedback to individual student profiles
based on continuous engagement monitoring \parencite{Kwakye2025}.

\subsection{Machine Learning Algorithms and Techniques}

\subsubsection{Machine Learning Algorithms}
Machine Learning especially its type of Supervised learning,
is the most widely adopted paradigm in EDM,
primarily applied to classification tasks---predicting categorical
outcomes such as pass/fail, dropout/persist, or risk level---and
regression tasks that produce continuous scores \parencite{Bello2025,
Dirin2022}. Three families of supervised learning algorithms are
prominent in the EDM literature: ensemble methods, deep learning
models, and traditional classifiers.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Ensemble Methods such as Random Forest, XGBoost, and Gradient
    Boosting are consistently identified as top performers in EDM contexts
    due to their ability to manage complex, non-linear relationships in
    educational data more effectively than baseline linear models. Bello
    et al. (2025) demonstrated this directly: on a dataset of 1,163 student
    records, a baseline Linear Regression model achieved an R\textsuperscript{2}
    of only .32, while Random Forest achieved .69 and XGBoost achieved .70,
    confirming the superior generalisation capacity of ensemble methods
    \parencite{Bello2025}.

    \item Deep Learning models---including Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP),
    Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
    networks---are increasingly utilised to capture intricate temporal
    interactions between features. While these architectures often achieve
    high predictive accuracy, they operate as ``black boxes,'' providing
    limited insight into the reasoning behind individual predictions, which
    significantly limits their institutional adoption in advisory contexts
    \parencite{Kwakye2025, Aldowah2026}.

    \item Traditional Classifiers including Decision Trees, Support
    Vector Machines (SVM), Na\"{i}ve Bayes, and Logistic Regression remain
    widely used for their relative simplicity, computational efficiency,
    and inherent interpretability. Their performance, however, is generally
    outpaced by ensemble methods when the underlying data contains
    non-linear feature interactions \parencite{Bello2025}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Techniques}
Selecting the right algorithm is necessary but insufficient; the
techniques governing how that algorithm is trained and validated
determine whether its performance generalises reliably to unseen
students. Of the three algorithm families reviewed above, ensemble
methods in particular require careful deployment discipline---their
capacity to fit complex non-linear patterns makes them equally capable
of overfitting when training and evaluation procedures are not
rigorously controlled. Standard practice in EDM therefore applies
four core techniques to ensure that reported performance reflects
genuine generalisation rather than dataset-specific memorisation
\parencite{Bello2025, Aldowah2026}.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stratified train/test splitting} preserves the empirical
    class ratio across training and holdout partitions, preventing
    artificially optimistic evaluation on imbalanced datasets.
    \item \textbf{$k$-fold cross-validation} assesses generalisation across
    multiple data subsets rather than a single holdout, producing more
    stable performance estimates.
    \item \textbf{SMOTE} (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) addresses
    the class imbalance characteristic of student risk datasets---where
    at-risk students constitute a minority---by generating synthetic
    minority-class instances during training.
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameter tuning} optimises each algorithm's
    configuration prior to final evaluation, ensuring that reported
    performance reflects a properly calibrated model rather than
    default settings \parencite{Bello2025, Aldowah2026}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Data Sources and Feature Engineering}

\subsubsection{Data Sources}
A data source is any institutional system that generates raw
student records---such as grade histories, login activity, or attendance
logs---that can be extracted for predictive modelling. Without reliable
and comprehensive data sources, no algorithm or engineering procedure
can produce valid risk predictions. ML models in EDM rely on three
primary institutional data sources: Academic Management Systems, which
supply enrollment records, GPA trajectories, and academic standing
data; Learning Management Systems, which contribute behavioral
engagement signals including login frequency, resource access, and
assignment submission patterns; and clickstream logs from digital
platforms, which capture fine-grained temporal interaction data
\parencite{Kwakye2025}. Together, these sources provide the longitudinal,
multi-dimensional student record that predictive models require.

\subsubsection{Feature Engineering}
Raw institutional records cannot be fed directly into predictive models;
they must first be transformed into structured, model-ready input
variables that meaningfully represent student behaviour and academic
trajectory. This transformation process is \textbf{feature engineering},
and it constitutes the critical intermediate step between data
extraction and model training. Typical engineered features include
attendance rates, prior grade trajectories, assignment submission
timeliness, and temporal engagement patterns.

Research has established that \textit{time-based features}---such as
the intervals between LMS interactions, the recency of last login, and
the ``burstiness'' of activity relative to assignment deadlines---carry
significant predictive power, often capturing early disengagement signals
that static features such as a single GPA value cannot detect
\parencite{Dirin2022}. To ensure model stability and address the class
imbalance that characterises most student risk datasets---where at-risk
students constitute a minority---standard pre-processing techniques
including normalisation, one-hot encoding, and the Synthetic Minority
Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) are routinely applied
\parencite{Bello2025, Aldowah2026}.

\subsection{Explainability, Fairness, and Ethical Considerations}

Three foundational concepts structure this subsection.
\textbf{Explainability} refers to the capacity of a predictive model
to provide human-interpretable reasons for its outputs---enabling
advisors and administrators to understand, interrogate, and defend
risk classifications. Fairness refers to the requirement that
model predictions do not systematically disadvantage students on the
basis of protected or socioeconomic characteristics. Ethical
considerations encompass the broader governance obligations---including
data privacy, accountability, and human oversight---that arise when
algorithmic outputs are used to make or inform decisions affecting
students' academic futures. As ML becomes embedded in institutional
decision-making, all three have emerged as critical prerequisites for
responsible deployment \parencite{Kwakye2025, Bello2025}.

\subsubsection{Explainability and the Black Box Problem}
Complex ensemble and deep learning models produce predictions without
human-interpretable reasoning, creating an institutional trust deficit
that prevents adoption by advisors and administrators who cannot
interrogate or defend the model's outputs \parencite{Kwakye2025}.
To address this, researchers now employ XAI (Explainable AI)
techniques---most prominently SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations),
LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), and Partial
Dependence Plots (PDP)---to provide feature-level explanations for
individual predictions \parencite{Kwakye2025}. A Springer study
applying CatBoost--SHAP to student academic crisis warning demonstrated
that explainable predictions provide intuitive decision support and
guidance assistance for education administrators, validating SHAP's
role as a trust-enabling layer in institutional deployments
\parencite{Zhang2021}.

\subsubsection{Fairness and Bias}
Predictive models trained on historically biased institutional data
risk reinforcing systemic inequalities, particularly for students from
disadvantaged socioeconomic backgrounds. Without deliberate fairness
auditing, a model may achieve high aggregate accuracy while
systematically misclassifying vulnerable subpopulations---a failure
mode that is especially consequential when model outputs determine
which students receive scarce advising and support resources
\parencite{Bello2025}.

\subsubsection{Ethical Considerations}
Two interrelated ethical obligations govern institutional deployment
of student analytics systems. First, human-in-the-loop
deployment requires that ML models function as decision-support
tools that complement rather than replace human pedagogical judgment
\parencite{Kwakye2025}. The advisor remains the responsible agent; the
model supplies evidence. This principle directly informs the role-based
dashboard architecture of this study, where SHAP outputs are presented
as advisor-facing explanations rather than autonomous risk verdicts.
Second, data privacy requires compliance with data protection
frameworks---including GDPR in European contexts and equivalent national
regulations---mandating robust data anonymisation, access controls, and
transparent data governance policies \parencite{Kwakye2025}.

\subsection{Existing ML-based predictive analytics for at-risk students}

\subsubsection{Georgia State University: GPS Advising System}

\textbf{Institutional Context and Motivation}

Georgia State University (GSU) is a large public research university in
Atlanta \parencite{GSU2024}. It enrolls more than 50,000 students---of whom 58\%
qualify as low-income by federal standards, 60\% are non-white, and 30\%
are first-generation college students---provides the most extensively
documented and empirically evaluated implementation of ML-based predictive
analytics for student retention in higher education
\parencite{GSU2024, Renick2017}. Prior to 2012, GSU's annual dropout rate
stood at approximately 5,600 students per year, with adviser-to-student
ratios as high as 1:1,000. Students---particularly those from minority and
low-income backgrounds---were failing and withdrawing without ever seeking
help, because the institution had no mechanism for identifying them before
failure had already occurred \parencite{NPR2016}.

\textbf{System Architecture and Methodology}

In 2011, GSU entered a partnership with the Education Advisory Board
(EAB)---a specialist higher education analytics consultancy---to develop
what would become the GPS (Graduation and Progression Success) Advising
system \parencite{NPR2016, Hechinger2019}. The methodology proceeded
in three phases:

\textbf{Phase 1 --- Historical Data Analysis:} EAB analysts processed
ten years of GSU institutional records, encompassing 2.5 million course
grades and 140,000 student records, to identify patterns statistically
associated with student attrition and programme delay
\parencite{NPR2016, GSUCaseStudy2019}.

\textbf{Phase 2 --- Risk Factor Development:} From this historical
analysis, a library of more than 800 risk markers was developed. These
markers span a wide range of behavioral and academic signals: course
registration anomalies (such as a senior enrolling in a course that does
not count toward their degree), grade patterns, financial account status,
LMS engagement changes, and attendance indicators derived from Wi-Fi and
LMS login monitoring \parencite{GSU2024, EdTech2019}. Risk markers range
from \textit{repeated historical patterns}---behaviours that have
historically preceded dropout across thousands of prior students---to
\textit{specific points of change}, such as a sudden drop in grades or a
significant reduction in LMS usage \parencite{GSU2024}.

\textbf{Phase 3 --- Operational Deployment:} The GPS system went live in
August 2012. It operates as a nightly batch process: student grades,
records, and behavioral indicators are updated every night, and the system
generates push-notification alerts to the assigned academic adviser
whenever a student triggers one or more risk markers
\parencite{GSUCaseStudy2019, NPR2016}. Advisers are expected to initiate
contact with the flagged student within 48 hours \parencite{EdTech2019}.
The system is delivered through EAB's proprietary web platform,
\textit{Navigate}, which presents each student's risk score on a
color-coded dashboard (green/yellow/red) alongside the specific alert
triggers \parencite{Signal2019}.

\textbf{Outcomes and Impact}

The results of GPS Advising are among the most cited in the student
success literature. Since its launch, GSU has recorded the following
verified outcomes \parencite{GSU2024, GSUCaseStudy2019, NISS2024}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Graduation rates increased by 23 percentage points over the
    decade following implementation, with four-year graduation rates
    specifically improving by 7.8 percentage points.
    \item The system generates more than 90,000 adviser-initiated
    interventions per academic year, compared to approximately 1,000
    annual adviser meetings before 2012.
    \item Over 250,000 one-on-one meetings between advisers and students
    have been prompted by GPS alerts since 2012.
    \item Achievement gaps based on race, ethnicity, and income level
    have been eliminated: Black, Hispanic, first-generation, and
    low-income students now graduate at rates at or above the overall
    student body rate---the only national university in the United States
    to have achieved this outcome.
    \item A Boston Consulting Group analysis confirmed that the programme
    generates more revenue than it costs, with each one-percentage-point
    increase in retention generating approximately \$3.18 million in
    additional tuition revenue annually \parencite{Hechinger2019}.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Limitations and Critical Assessment}

\textbf{Proprietary and Non-Transferable Architecture:} The most
consequential limitation is that GPS Advising is built on EAB's
proprietary \textit{Navigate} platform, for which GSU paid a heavily
discounted rate of \$150,000 per year as an early adopter
\parencite{Hechinger2019}. Market-rate licensing costs are substantially
higher for most institutions in the Global South,
including Kenyan universities operating under severe resource constraints.
This creates a fundamental equity problem: the institutions most in need
of student success technology are precisely those least able to afford
commercial vendor solutions.

\textbf{Lack of Model Transparency:} Because the underlying algorithmic
logic of the \textit{Navigate} platform is proprietary, institutions
cannot inspect, audit, or modify the predictive models. This opacity
prevents the kind of institution-specific calibration that this study
argues is essential: Strathmore University's 67\% attendance threshold
and 2.0 GPA floor require that predictive models be explicitly tuned
to local policy parameters, which is impossible within a black-box
commercial system \parencite{Hechinger2019, Signal2019}.

\textbf{Resource-Intensive Implementation:} GSU's success was not
solely the product of technology. More than 90\% of the total programme
cost was in staffing: GSU hired 42 new academic advisers in the first
six months, reducing the adviser-to-student ratio from 1:1,000 to
approximately 1:300 \parencite{NPR2016}. This level of staffing
investment is not replicable in resource-constrained contexts, and
it raises the question of whether the technology or the additional
human capacity was the primary driver of improved outcomes.

\textbf{Relevance to This Study}

The Georgia State case establishes two foundational propositions for
this research. First, it provides proof of concept at institutional
scale: predictive analytics, when properly implemented and supported
by human advisory capacity, can dramatically improve retention and
eliminate equity gaps \parencite{GSU2024}. Second, and equally
importantly, its limitations define the design requirements for the
current study. The Strathmore tool intended to be developed in this thesis is therefore not
simply inspired by the Georgia State model---it is explicitly designed
to resolve the transferability and transparency gaps that prevent that
model from being deployed in a Kenyan institutional context.

\subsubsection{Morocco: A Modular and Explainable Machine Learning Pipeline at Mohammed V University}

\textbf{Institutional Context and Motivation}

While the Georgia State GPS Advising system demonstrates the institutional
impact achievable through predictive analytics at scale, its proprietary
architecture and resource requirements limit its transferability to
institutions in the Global South. A critically important and more recent
contribution to the EDM literature---directly relevant to the African
higher education context---is a modular dropout prediction pipeline
developed at the Mohammadia School of Engineers, Mohammed V University
in Rabat, Morocco, by Bettahi, Belouadha, and Harroud (2025). This study
is geographically proximate to the Kenyan context, uses open-source tools,
and explicitly addresses the governance and explainability requirements
that the GSU system does not \parencite{Bettahi2025}.

\textbf{Dataset and Institutional Setting}

The study used a longitudinal institutional dataset of 17,883 undergraduate
records drawn from multiple cohorts at a Moroccan HEI. After cleaning and
feature curation, the modeling matrix comprised 91 features integrating
four categories of student data: demographic and programme descriptors;
academic progression variables (cumulative GPA, credits earned, course
failures, and passed course counts); engagement and attendance metrics;
and financial and administrative signals encoded as binary hold flags
(\texttt{HAS\_*} variables) with paired timestamp durations. The positive
class---students recorded by the registrar as having discontinued their
studies---comprised 2,856 students (16.0\%), producing a substantially
imbalanced classification problem typical of real institutional data
\parencite{Bettahi2025}.

\textbf{Methodology and Pipeline Architecture}

The pipeline was implemented entirely in Python 3.12.0 using
open-source libraries, specifically scikit-learn 1.7.1,
xgboost 3.0.3, and shap 0.48.0. This is a significant
methodological distinction from the proprietary EAB \textit{Navigate}
platform used by Georgia State: every component of the Moroccan pipeline
is reproducible, auditable, and transferable without licensing costs
\parencite{Bettahi2025}.

The six-stage modular architecture proceeded as follows
\parencite{Bettahi2025}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Ingestion and Cleaning:} Automatic delimiter
    detection; removal of columns with $>90\%$ missingness; median
    imputation for numeric fields; label encoding for categorical variables
    with an explicit <MISSING> token; date normalization to
    elapsed days; and leakage prevention by dropping direct student
    identifiers.

    \item \textbf{Feature Engineering:} Transformation of raw institutional
    records into 91 structured predictors, including temporal features such
    as hold durations and derived academic momentum indicators.

    \item \textbf{Train/Test Split:} A stratified 80/20 split yielding
    14,306 training and 3,577 test students, preserving the empirical
    class ratio in both partitions.

    \item \textbf{Model Benchmarking:} Nine algorithms were evaluated:
    Logistic Regression (LR), Decision Tree (DT), Random Forest (RF),
    k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), Gradient
    Boosting (GB), XGBoost, Na\"{i}ve Bayes (NB), and Multilayer
    Perceptron (MLP).

    \item \textbf{Explainability Layer (SHAP):} SHAP TreeExplainer
    applied to all tree-based models on the full 3,577-test-case set;
    KernelExplainer with $k$-means background applied to
    MLP on a 300-sample subset.

    \item \textbf{Reporting and Visualization:} Structured output
    directories storing confusion matrices, ROC curves, SHAP plots,
    performance summaries, and reproducibility logs.
\end{enumerate}

Model validation used stratified 5-fold cross-validation with a fixed
random seed (42). A single operating threshold $\tau$ was selected per
model by maximizing F1 on cross-validation folds and then fixed for
all test and subgroup reporting \parencite{Bettahi2025}.

\textbf{Limitation Critical Assessment}

The Bettahi et al. pipeline is the most methodologically aligned existing
system in the literature relative to the platform developed in this
thesis. It validates XGBoost and SHAP as the dominant tools for
structured, imbalanced HEI dropout prediction using entirely open-source
infrastructure---directly addressing the transferability limitation of
the Georgia State model \parencite{Bettahi2025}. The use of Python with
open-source libraries (\texttt{scikit-learn}, \texttt{xgboost},
\texttt{shap}) establishes the technical feasibility of deploying a
high-performance, explainable EWS without commercial licensing, while
the SHAP-based risk versus protective factor distinction provides the
explainability architecture template for the role-based advisor
dashboards developed in Chapter Four.

However, the study carries two significant limitations that this thesis
directly addresses. First, the institution-specific features central to
the pipeline---particularly administrative timestamp variables such as
\texttt{AP\_START\_DTE} and \texttt{AP\_END\_DTE}---are Moroccan
HEI-specific and carry no equivalent semantics in other institutional
contexts, limiting the direct transferability of the feature engineering
strategy to universities with different administrative infrastructures
\parencite{Bettahi2025}. This motivates this study's institution-specific
feature engineering, which encodes Strathmore's 67\% attendance threshold
and 2.0 GPA floor as policy-calibrated variables rather than generic
proxies. Second, the pipeline predicts a single binary dropout outcome
only, failing to capture the graduated spectrum of academic difficulty
that students actually experience. This study departs from that binary
framing through the simultaneous prediction of three distinct risk
outcomes---dropout risk, course failure risk, and programme delay
risk---with all models explicitly calibrated to Strathmore University's
specific policy thresholds.

\subsubsection{South Africa: Machine Learning for Engineering Student Success at the University of Johannesburg}

One of the earliest investigations of ML-based student performance
prediction in the South African context was conducted by Taodzera, Twala,
and Carroll at the University of Johannesburg's Department of Electrical
and Electronics Engineering Science \parencite{Taodzera2016}.

\textbf{Dataset and Features}

The study worked with 1,366 engineering student records drawn from a South African university. Available features spanned demographic and academic entry indicators: high school Mathematics score, Physics score, English score, Life Orientation score, ethnicity, home language, age, school province, and religion. The response variable was defined as the number of subjects passed to qualify for progression to second year---a binary pass/fail classification motivated by the institution's own progression regulations \parencite{Taodzera2016}.

\textbf{Feature Selection}

Correlation analysis identified Physics score (r = 0.2391) and Mathematics score (r = 0.1836) as the strongest predictors of first-year success, followed by age (r = 0.1191) and ethnicity (r = 0.0808). The significance of ethnicity as a predictor directly reflects structural inequalities in South African secondary schooling, where historically advantaged schools continue to produce students substantially better prepared for the quantitative demands of engineering study \parencite{Taodzera2016}. Wrapper-based feature selection confirmed Mathematics and Physics as the primary predictors, with ethnicity and school province also identified as significant.

\textbf{Models and Results}

Five algorithms were evaluated: Logistic Regression (LR), Na\"{i}ve Bayes (NB), Artificial Neural Network (ANN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), and Decision Tree (DT). The DT algorithm performed best with 65.86\% accuracy, an F1-score of 0.658, and a Kappa statistic of 0.316---classifying it as a fair predictor. SVM ranked second (64.63\%), followed by NB (63.41\%) and LR (63.17\%). ANN performed weakest (60.24\%) \parencite{Taodzera2016}.

\textbf{Limitation Critical Assessment}

The Taodzera et al. study makes two contributions of direct relevance to this thesis. First, it demonstrates that standard ML algorithms can be operationalized in a resource-limited African institutional setting using only enrollment-time features available at the point of admission---without requiring LMS integration, real-time data pipelines, or proprietary vendor platforms. Second, the identification of school province and ethnicity as statistically significant predictors highlights a structural feature of African educational data that does not appear in the North American literature: that secondary schooling inequality is itself a measurable risk factor that encodes into university-level performance data.

However, the absence of SHAP-based or any form of explainability analysis means the model cannot be operationalized as a policy-actionable early warning system. Furthermore, the modest Kappa values (all below 0.32) suggest that all five models classify at a level only slightly above what chance would produce \parencite{Taodzera2016}.

\subsubsection{South Africa: Bayesian Network Classification for Computer Science Student Risk Prediction at the University of Cape Town}

At the University of Cape Town's Department of Computer Science, Hassen conducted a study applying Bayesian Network classifiers to predict which second-year Computer Science students that were at risk of failing CSC2001F \parencite{Hassen2020}. This was a core data structures course widely regarded as the critical attrition point in the UCT Computer Science major.

\textbf{Dataset and Features}

The study drew on 728 student records spanning six cohorts from 2013 to 2019. Features were selected through iterative consultation with the course convenor and spanned both pre-entry and early in-course indicators: Matric Mathematics mark, Matric English mark, National Benchmark Test (NBT) scores in Mathematics, Quantitative Literacy and Academic Literacy, first-year Computer Science course final marks (CSC1015F and CSC1016S), the first assignment mark for CSC2001F, and the CSC2001F Test 1 mark \parencite{Hassen2020}.

\textbf{Methodological Design}

Three Bayesian Network models were developed using the Netica platform. The first was manually constructed from expert knowledge gathered in faculty consultations. The second was generated automatically using Netica's Tree Augmented Na\"{i}ve Bayes (TAN) structure learning algorithm. The third was a combined model synthesising insights from both. A notable methodological contribution was the adoption of incremental training, wherein each cohort's model was trained exclusively on all preceding years' data and evaluated on the subsequent cohort --- replicating the temporal constraints of genuine deployment \parencite{Hassen2020}.

\textbf{Models and Results}

The combined model produced the most instructive outcome: while its accuracy (83.33\%) was marginally below the TAN model's, it achieved a perfect recall score of 1.0 --- correctly identifying every at-risk student in the test cohort --- alongside an F1 score of 0.40 and a precision of 0.25 \parencite{Hassen2020}.

\textbf{Limitation Critical Assessment}

The Hassen study validates incremental cohort-based training as a deployment-realistic evaluation strategy and establishes recall as the theoretically appropriate primary metric in student support contexts \parencite{Hassen2020}. However, the dataset of 728 records is small by ML standards, and the absence of any SHAP-based explainability means the model cannot generate student-level explanations sufficient to guide individualised advising conversations.

\subsubsection{Ethiopia: Predictive Analytics at Scale with African Institutional Data}

A more recent study from the East African context was conducted by Esmael (2024) at Wollo University and the Kombolcha Institute of Technology in Ethiopia \parencite{Esmael2024}. The dataset comprised 32,582 student records from academic years 2017--2022. SVM achieved the highest post-tuning accuracy of 96.0\% (precision 0.94, recall 0.98, Kappa 0.94), followed by Decision Tree at 93.4\%. The Esmael study confirms that hyperparameter tuning is a critical technique for improving model performance on educational datasets, directly validating the tuning strategy adopted in this study. However, the absence of any explainability layer means that model outputs cannot be translated into student-specific intervention recommendations \parencite{Esmael2024}.

\subsubsection{Kenya: Business Intelligence and Decision Tree Classification for Student Retention Prediction --- case of Machakos University College}

One of the few published East African studies applying machine learning techniques to student retention prediction in a higher education context was conducted by Ngemu, Odongo, Omulo, and Manderick, drawing on data from Machakos University College in Kenya \parencite{Ngemu2015}. The J48 decision tree outperformed all competitors, achieving a cross-validation accuracy of 94.8\% and a recall of 94.4\%. The dominance of fees payment difficulty as the single most informative predictor --- with a gain ratio of 0.424 --- positions financial exclusion as the primary mechanism of student attrition in this Kenyan institutional setting \parencite{Ngemu2015}. However, the dataset of 270 instances is extremely small, and the study provides no class imbalance handling, no SHAP-based explainability, and no longitudinal or cohort-based evaluation.

\subsubsection*{Conceptual Framework Explanation}

The solution architecture presented in the above conceptual framework diagram
describes an end-to-end pipeline through which raw institutional data is
transformed into actionable, explainable risk predictions for Strathmore
University's academic support staff. The framework flows strictly from
top to bottom, with each stage building on the output of the preceding one.

\textbf{Stage 1 --- Authentication:} The pipeline originates from
Active Directory, which governs authenticated access to the three
institutional source systems, ensuring that only authorised data flows
enter the pipeline.

\textbf{Stage 2 --- Data Sources:} Three institutional systems operate
in parallel: the Academic Management System (AMS), which supplies
enrollment records, GPA trajectories, and academic standing data; the
Learning Management System (LMS), which contributes behavioral
engagement signals including login frequency, resource access, and
assignment submission patterns; and the Attendance Tracking Platform,
which provides real-time attendance rates and flags students approaching
the mandatory 67\% examination eligibility threshold.

\textbf{Stage 3 --- Data Pipeline:} Raw data extracted from the three
source systems is passed through an integrated data pipeline that merges
records across systems, resolves inconsistencies, and validates data
integrity before any modelling takes place.

\textbf{Stage 4 --- Feature Engineering:} The cleaned dataset is
transformed into a structured feature matrix. This stage applies
normalisation, categorical encoding, SMOTE oversampling to address
class imbalance, and the construction of derived indicators such as
attendance breach flags, GPA floor proximity scores, and temporal
engagement patterns.

\textbf{Stage 5 --- Model Training:} Three ensemble algorithms---Random
Forest, XGBoost, and Gradient Boosting---are trained on the engineered
feature matrix using historical student cohort data from 2021 to 2024.
Stratified cross-validation and hyperparameter tuning are applied to
maximise generalisation performance.

\textbf{Stage 6 --- Prediction Engine:} The trained models generate
three simultaneous risk scores for each currently enrolled student:
dropout risk, course failure risk, and programme delay risk. This
multi-outcome architecture reflects the graduated spectrum of academic
difficulty that students experience, extending beyond the binary dropout
classification common in prior literature.

\textbf{Stage 7 --- SHAP Explainability:} All three risk scores
converge into the SHAP explainability layer, which decomposes each
prediction into its contributing feature drivers, identifying both risk
factors and protective factors at the individual student level. This
transforms opaque model outputs into human-interpretable explanations
that advisors can act on.

\textbf{Stage 8 --- Institutional Actors:} The pipeline terminates
with the human actors who consume the system's outputs through
role-based dashboards: the University Administrator, the School
Administrator, and the Academic Mentor or Advisor. This
human-in-the-loop design ensures that the platform functions as a
decision-support tool that augments rather than replaces professional
academic judgment.

Taken together, the framework operationalises the transition from
Strathmore University's current reactive support paradigm---where
students are identified only after breaching academic thresholds---to
a proactive, evidence-driven intervention model in which at-risk
students are flagged months or weeks before failure occurs, and advisors are
equipped with the specific reasons behind each risk classification.

\subsection*{Summary}
This chapter established the theoretical and empirical foundations for the
study's predictive analytics platform. Global attrition statistics confirmed
student dropout as a structurally significant challenge, while Tinto's Student
Integration Model and Astin's I-E-O Model provided the conceptual framework mapping
institutional data variables to retention outcomes. The review of existing ML-based
systems --- spanning Georgia State University, Morocco, South Africa, Ethiopia, and
Kenya --- demonstrated that ensemble methods, particularly XGBoost and Random Forest,
consistently outperform classical algorithms, while SHAP-based explainability remains
critically underutilized in African contexts. All gaps identified directly inform the platform's design.
Chapter Three now presents the research methodology, detailing the data collection strategy,
feature engineering process, model development approach, and evaluation framework employed
to build and validate the system.
