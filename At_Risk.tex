\chapter{INTRODUCTION}


\section{Background to the Study}

Student retention and academic success represent critical challenges confronting higher
 education institutions worldwide. According to \parencite{NCES2024}, undergraduate enrollment
  in degree-granting institutions decreased by 13\% between 2012 and 2022, while 23.3\% of
   undergraduate students leave universities annually. Six-year graduation rates reveal 
   substantial variation: 63\% at public universities, 68\% at private nonprofit institutions,
    and only 29\% at private for-profit institutions. This persistent attrition represents 
    significant personal and economic costs for individual students, alongside substantial 
    challenges for institutional sustainability and mission fulfillment.

Technology is changing that. Machine learning algorithms can now identify at-risk 
students weeks or even months before traditional warning signs appear, achieving 
prediction accuracies of 88–92\% \parencite{Ahmed2024, Gathani2026}. Georgia State
 University's GPS Advising system illustrates what becomes possible: by tracking 
 over 800 risk factors for 40,000 students daily and generating 90,000 targeted
  interventions each year, the university raised its graduation rate by 7.8 percentage
   points and virtually eliminated achievement gaps between demographic groups
    \parencite{GSU2024}. The technology, in short, is mature and proven—but
     its benefits have not yet reached most of the world.

Africa is a striking example of that gap. The African Union's Continental 
Education Strategy (CESA 2016–2025) acknowledges retention as a priority, 
yet most African universities still rely on end-of-semester transcripts as 
their primary diagnostic tool. Some sub-regions have begun to experiment. Mohammed V University in Morocco 
achieved an 81.66\% dropout-prediction accuracy in a pilot study that utilized L2-regularized logistic 
regression \parencite{Bouihi2024}. "Similarly, a study at the University of Johannesburg 
in South Africa utilized machine learning to predict the performance of 1,366 engineering students, 
achieving a 65.86\% accuracy rate using Decision Tree algorithms and identifying prior performance in
 physics and mathematics as primary success factors \parencite{taodzera2}.

Within East Africa, Kenya's higher education sector faces particularly acute retention pressures. 
Government capitation covers only 57\% of students against an 80\% target, while public universities
 have accumulated Ksh 62 billion in unpaid bills—structural constraints that make student attrition
  both more likely and more financially consequential for students, families, and institutions 
  \parencite{KIPPRA2024, DailyNation2024}. 
  
  Recognizing the challenge of student attrition, researchers 
  from the University of Nairobi in collaboration with one researcher at the Free University of Brussels conducted a study utilizing Machakos
   University College as a case study to apply Business Intelligence and machine learning to dropout
    prediction \parencite{ngemu2015}. Using the WEKA toolkit to analyze historical institutional data, 
    the research team—comprising Ngemu, Odongo, Omulo, and Manderick—evaluated multiple classification 
    algorithms, including Naïve Bayes, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and the J48 decision tree.
     Their findings identified that students at-risk  of dropping out could be accurately flagged based on critical 
     predictors such as financial stability (fee payment), entry age, health status, and parental 
     occupation \parencite{ngemu2015}.


Strathmore University operates within this challenging global landscape. While maintaining distinct academic 
standards that intensify the consequences of delayed intervention. The institution enforces a 67\% minimum
 attendance threshold for examination eligibility and a 2.0 GPA floor for good academic standing—policy
  thresholds that render early identification especially critical, as students who breach them may be 
  barred from examinations or placed on academic probation regardless of their potential for recovery. 
  The university collects extensive student data through its Academic Management System (AMS), Learning 
  Management System (LMS), and attendance tracking platform, generating daily records on academic performance, 
  engagement behaviour, and class participation. 
  
  Despite this data richness, these systems operate as isolated 
  administrative repositories with no integrated analytics capability, preventing the transformation of raw
   institutional data into actionable early warnings. Students are typically identified for intervention only
    after manifesting metrics confirming trends and outcomes after events have occured. This reactive paradigm, 
     though common across Kenyan institutions, is inconsistent both with international best practice
      (Georgia State University, 2024) and with Kenya's national call for greater technology utilisation in higher
       education (Republic of Kenya Ministry of Education, 2024). This study addresses that gap directly: by developing 
       a machine learning-based predictive analytics platform calibrated to Strathmore's academic policies, data 
       infrastructure, and resource constraints, it seeks to transform the institution's support model from reactive
        monitoring to proactive, evidence-driven intervention.

\section{Statement of the Problem}

Strathmore University currently faces a critical gap between data collection and actionable 
student support. Although the institution maintains robust data streams through its Academic
 Management System (AMS), Learning Management System (LMS), and attendance tracking platforms, 
 these systems operate as isolated administrative repositories rather than integrated
  predictive tools. This fragmentation prevents the university from transforming raw 
  institutional data into early warnings for at-risk undergraduate students.

The primary consequence of this fragmentation is a reactive support paradigm that 
carries measurable human costs. Students are typically identified for intervention 
only after manifesting indicators of failure---cumulative low grades, breach of 
the mandatory 67\% attendance threshold for examination eligibility, or formal 
withdrawal notice. By this stage, the window for effective academic recovery has 
often closed, and the outcomes that follow are both predictable and preventable: 
students drop out of programmes they could have completed, fail courses that timely 
tutoring referrals could have salvaged, and accumulate delays that extend their 
studies by entire semesters---each outcome representing a personal and institutional 
loss that earlier detection would have averted.

Despite possessing the necessary digital infrastructure and longitudinal student 
datasets, Strathmore University lacks a specialised machine learning architecture 
capable of integrating these data streams into a coherent risk 
stratification system. This absence is consequential: without the capability to 
detect deteriorating engagement trajectories weeks before they manifest as failed 
grades or breached attendance thresholds, the institution cannot intervene at the 
point when intervention is still effective. Without an automated ML-based tool to predict 
dropout risk, course failure risk, and program delay risk simultaneously, the 
university remains unable to allocate its limited counselling and mentoring resources 
to the students who need them most---and advisors are left to act on intuition 
rather than evidence, on crisis rather than prevention.

This study therefore develops, implements, and evaluates an integrated predictive analytics 
ML-based tool tailored to Strathmore University's academic policies, data infrastructure, 
and resource constraints. By producing explainable and actionable risk predictions across
 three distinct outcomes, the ML-based tool is designed to transition the institution from 
 reactive to proactive monitoring and data-driven intervention.

\section{Justification}
\subsection{Operational Necessity}
This research is justified by the need to bridge the gap between data collection and data 
utilization in Universities such as Strathmore University. Currently, Strathmore's student support is
reactive—interventions. It often occurs only after a student has already failed or dropped out.
 There is an urgent need for a system that transforms underutilized data into actionable early
 warnings without increasing the administrative burden on faculty.
\subsection{Addressing the "Black Box" Problem}
This study is justified by its focus on explainability. By using specialized techniques to show why a 
student is at risk, the research justifies the shift toward evidence-based mentoring, ensuring that 
 institutional support is credible.
\subsection{Policy and Economic Pressures}
This study responds to the Kenya Ministry of Education’s Sessional Paper No. 1 of 2024, which cites 
inadequate technology use as a core bottleneck in higher education \parencite{KIPPRA2024}. In a climate
 of rising enrollments and limited government funding, the status quo of universal mentoring is no 
 longer sustainable. This research is justified as an economic necessity to ensure that finite counseling 
 resources are directed only where they are most needed.

\section{Scope and Delimitations of the Study}

\subsection{Scope}
This research focuses on the development and evaluation of a predictive analytics platform 
for approximately 5,000 undergraduate students across all five schools at Strathmore University,
 Kenya. The technical scope involves integrating data from the Academic Management System 
 (AMS), Learning Management System (LMS), and attendance tracking records into a unified 
 analytics pipeline. The study develops three prediction models—targeting dropout risk, 
 course failure risk, and program delay risk—using ensemble algorithms including Random 
 Forest, Gradient Boosting, and XGBoost, trained on historical student cohort data from 2021-2024 with known academic outcomes. Model predictions are accompanied by SHAP-based explainability outputs to ensure advisors and administrators can understand and act on the reasons behind each risk classification \parencite{Lundberg2017}. The resulting platform includes role-based dashboards for university administrators, school administrators, and academic mentors, with model performance measured through standard machine learning evaluation metrics (Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC) and platform effectiveness assessed through structured stakeholder feedback.

\subsection{Delimitations}
The research boundaries are defined by the following deliberate constraints. Regarding data factors, the analysis is restricted to academic risk factors measurable through institutional systems, such as attendance rates, GPA, LMS engagement, and course grades. External variables such as socioeconomic background, mental health status, and family circumstances are excluded, as they are not systematically captured in Strathmore's existing data infrastructure and their collection would raise ethical concerns beyond the current scope. In terms of student population, the study is limited to undergraduate students across Strathmore University's five schools; postgraduate and continuing education cohorts are excluded due to their unique assessment patterns. 

The system capabilities are delimited to identifying risk levels and suggesting general intervention categories, such as academic counselling or tutoring referrals, rather than providing course-specific remediation. Within the algorithmic scope, the study employs ensemble tree-based methods like Random Forest and XGBoost \parencite{Chen2016} rather than deep learning architectures. This decision is justified by the dataset size of approximately 5,000 students and the critical need for feature interpretability. Furthermore, the technical platform is developed as a web-based application, with mobile-native development remaining outside the scope. Financial domains, including fee payment risk and scholarship management, are also excluded to focus purely on academic and engagement indicators. Finally, the implementation phase concludes with a functional prototype and structured evaluation; full-scale production deployment and long-term longitudinal impact assessment are reserved for future research.

\subsection{Theoretical and Methodological Significance}
This study advances the field of Educational Data Mining (EDM) by operationalizing Tinto’s Student Integration Model within an
 African institutional context. It provides empirical evidence that Western retention theories can be mapped to quantifiable 
 data elements. Methodologically, it demonstrates that SHAP-based explainability is not a 
 luxury but a prerequisite for AI adoption in resource-constrained environments, setting a new standard for how predictive 
 models should be evaluated beyond simple accuracy.
\subsection{Practical Significance}
\textbf{For Institutions:} The study provides a documented, open-source blueprint for transitioning from reactive to proactive support. 
It offers a sustainable alternative to the individual-dependency models that have failed in other regional universities by 
embedding automated data pipelines into the university’s core infrastructure.

\textbf{For Educators:} The platform empowers advisors with role-based dashboards that move decision-making from intuition to evidence.
 It transforms the advisor’s role from a monitor to a facilitator, allowing for substantive, reason-led conversations

(like the 67\% attendance rule) into supportive frameworks, allowing for academic recovery before thresholds are irreversibly 
breached.

At the national level, this research serves as a replicable model for the Republic of Kenya’s transition to Competency-Based 
Education and Training (CBET). By demonstrating how a mid-sized university can implement high-level analytics within tight 
budget constraints, the study provides a policy instrument for other East African institutions to improve retention rates 
without the prohibitive costs of commercial software.

\section{Global Perspectives on Student Attrition}

Student retention is a global imperative linked directly to both institutional 
sustainability and the fulfillment of higher education's social mission. 
According to \textcite{NCES2024}, undergraduate enrollment in degree-granting 
institutions in the United States decreased by 13\% between 2012 and 2022, 
compounded by a persistent annual attrition rate of 23.3\%. Six-year graduation 
rates reveal substantial structural disparities: 68\% at private nonprofit 
institutions, 63\% at public universities, and only 29\% at private for-profit 
institutions. Despite a modest recent recovery---the 2024 National Student 
Clearinghouse report recorded a national first-year retention rate of 68\%, 
described as the highest in the past decade \parencite{NSC2024}---the 
structural problem of attrition remains unresolved, with large proportions of 
students continuing to exit before degree completion.

These figures carry dual consequences. At the institutional level, each 
departing student represents lost tuition revenue, wasted administrative 
investment, and diminished institutional capacity to fulfill its educational 
mission. At the individual level, students who withdraw often exit the system 
bearing financial debt without the credential necessary for socioeconomic 
mobility. A systematic review of 125 peer-reviewed studies on undergraduate 
retention published between 2014 and 2022 concluded that, despite decades of 
research and institutional intervention, student dropout remains a persistent 
and structurally significant problem across higher education globally, with 
research in the field remaining fragmented and without a unified evidence base 
for effective remediation \parencite{Goncalves2024}. The following table

 \section{Theoretical Frameworks for Student Retention}
 
\subsection{Tinto's Student Integration Model}

The dominant theoretical anchor for student retention research remains Vincent 
Tinto's Student Integration Model (SIM). First proposed by Tinto and 
Cullen in 1973 and formally published as a peer-reviewed synthesis in 1975, the 
model draws its conceptual foundations from Émile Durkheim's theory of suicide—
specifically the proposition that a deficit of social cohesion precipitates 
withdrawal from the community \parencite{Tinto1975}. Tinto's scholarly influence 
has been exceptional: his works have been cited in more than 700 studies across 
multiple disciplines, establishing the SIM as the most widely adopted
theoretical lens in higher education retention research \parencite{Bento2025}.

At its core, the SIM conceptualizes the university as a microcosm comprising two 
interdependent systems—the academic and the social—and posits that a student's 
persistence is governed by their degree of integration into both 
\parencite{Tinto1975, Tinto1993}. Critically, Tinto frames dropout not as a 
single discrete event but as a \textit{longitudinal process}: the student's 
commitment to persist is continuously reconfigured through ongoing interactions. This 
interactional character means that dropout cannot be attributed to any one 
variable in isolation; it emerges from the cumulative weight of how individual 
attributes, academic performance, and social relationships interact and 
accumulate over time \parencite{Tinto1993, Nicoletti2019}.

\subsubsection{The Longitudinal Mechanism}

The SIM operates through a structured, multi-stage sequence linking 
pre-institutional characteristics to eventual persistence:

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/TintoModel1.jpg}
    \caption{Tinto's Model of Institutional Departure}
    \label{fig:tinto_model}
\end{figure}

% Use [H] to force the figure to stay exactly here

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pre-Entry Attributes:} Students enter the institution carrying 
    three foundational background variables: family background, individual skills 
    and abilities, and prior schooling. These attributes do not directly determine 
    persistence but collectively shape the intellectual and social disposition the 
    student brings to the institution \parencite{Tinto1975}.

    \item \textbf{Initial Goals and Commitments:} Pre-entry attributes feed into 
    two parallel commitment structures. \textit{Intentions and goal commitments} 
    reflect the student's desire to earn a degree, while \textit{goal and 
    institutional commitments} reflect the desire to graduate from a specific 
    institution. Alongside these sits a third force---\textit{external 
    commitments}---representing obligations outside the institution, such as 
    family or employment, which compete with academic integration from the outset 
    \parencite{Tinto1975, Tinto1993}.

    \item \textbf{Institutional Experiences:} Once enrolled, students interact 
    simultaneously with two systems. The \textit{Academic System} operates 
    through both formal channels---academic performance---and informal 
    channels---faculty and staff interactions. The \textit{Social System} 
    operates through formal extracurricular activities and informal peer group 
    interactions. Both systems are embedded within and influenced by the broader 
    \textit{External Community} \parencite{Tinto1993}.

    \item \textbf{Integration:} The quality of institutional experiences 
    determines the degree of integration achieved along two dimensions. 
    \textit{Academic integration} results from engagement with the Academic 
    System, while \textit{social integration} results from engagement with the 
    Social System. These two dimensions are interdependent---weakness in either 
    increases the probability of departure \parencite{Tinto1975, Nicoletti2019}.

    \item \textbf{Re-evaluated Goals and Commitments:} Integration feeds back 
    into a revised set of commitments---updated intentions, goal and institutional 
    commitments, and external commitments---which are now reconfigured by the 
    student's actual lived experience within the institution. This re-evaluation 
    stage is what makes the model longitudinal and iterative rather than static: 
    commitments are not fixed at entry but continuously reshaped over time 
    \parencite{Nicoletti2019, Tinto1993}.

    \item \textbf{Departure Decision (Outcome):} The re-evaluated commitments 
    produce the final outcome: a \textit{departure decision}. If the cumulative 
    weight of academic and social integration has reinforced the student's goal 
    and institutional commitment, persistence follows. If integration has been 
    insufficient and external commitments or personal costs outweigh the perceived 
    benefits of continued enrollment, departure follows. Critically, the entire 
    process unfolds across time (\textit{t}), underscoring that dropout is a 
    process, not a moment \parencite{Tinto1975}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{The Two Pillars of Integration}

Tinto distinguishes two primary and interdependent dimensions of integration, 
both of which must be achieved to sustain persistence \parencite{Tinto1993}:

\textbf{Academic Integration} encompasses both structural and normative 
dimensions. Structural integration is measured by grade performance (GPA) 
relative to institutional standards, while normative integration reflects 
intellectual development and alignment with the academic community's values. 
Grades function not merely as performance records but as institutional signals 
of belonging within the academic system; a declining GPA is therefore not simply 
a performance metric but a symptom of deteriorating academic integration 
\parencite{Tinto1975, Bento2025}.

\textbf{Social Integration} reflects the quality of peer-group interactions and 
faculty relationships. Research on blended and online learning environments has 
confirmed that when instructors provided timely support, appropriate feedback, 
and interactive engagement, students were substantially more likely to persist 
\parencite{Ivankova2007}. Furthermore, studies measuring LMS engagement have 
established that completers consistently demonstrate significantly higher levels 
of participation in learning activities—measured through discussion posts, 
resource access, and login frequency—than withdrawers, validating these 
behavioral signals as digital proxies for integration \parencite{Morris2005}.

\subsubsection{Limitations and Critiques}

Despite its canonical status, the SIM carries limitations directly relevant to 
this study's context. Its key constructs—integration, commitment, fit—lack 
precise operational definitions, creating interpretive challenges when 
translating the model into computable variables \parencite{Nicoletti2019}. The 
model was also developed within a Western, residential university context and 
requires careful contextual recalibration before application in non-Western 
institutions such as Strathmore University. 
Additionally, traditional SIM-based research has relied predominantly on 
retrospective, correlational designs: the model identifies \textit{what} predicts 
departure but does not generate prospective, real-time predictions for individual 
students. This retrospective limitation is the precise gap that machine learning 
addresses—where the SIM identifies the risk factors, ML determines when those 
factors are currently activating for a specific student.

\subsubsection{Operationalization in This Study}

This study operationalizes Tinto's model within Strathmore's digital 
infrastructure by systematically mapping each theoretical construct onto a 
measurable institutional variable. The mapping is deliberate and bidirectional: 
every predictor variable selected for the ML models corresponds to a specific 
SIM construct, ensuring that algorithmic outputs retain theoretical 
interpretability.

\subsection{Astin's Input-Environment-Output (I-E-O) Model}

Alongside Tinto's SIM, Alexander Astin's Input-Environment-Output (I-E-O) 
model provides a complementary and practically powerful theoretical lens for 
this study. Originally developed as a conceptual guide for institutional 
assessment, the model was empirically consolidated in Astin's landmark study 
\textit{What Matters in College? Four Critical Years Revisited}, which drew on 
surveys of more than 20,000 students, 25,000 faculty members, and 200 
institutions to examine how academic programmes, faculty, peer groups, and 
institutional policies shape student development and outcomes 
\parencite{Astin1993}. The model's philosophical foundation is a 
\textit{talent development} approach to institutional excellence: quality is 
not measured by institutional prestige or resource endowment, but by the 
degree to which the institution succeeds in developing the potential of the 
students it admits \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. This orientation makes the 
I-E-O model especially relevant to the Strathmore context, where the goal is 
not to rank students but to identify and support those whose potential is at 
risk of being lost.

\subsubsection{Structure of the Model}

The I-E-O model organizes the variables relevant to student success into three 
sequential, interacting components \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/AstinModel.png}
    \caption{Astin’s input-environment-output (I-E-O) model (1993).}
    \label{fig:tinto_model}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inputs} refer to the personal qualities and characteristics 
    students bring with them at the point of entry. This includes demographic 
    attributes (age, sex), family background, prior academic achievement such 
    as secondary school GPA, and initial motivations and goals. Astin's 
    framework treats no single input as deterministic; rather, inputs establish 
    the baseline against which the effects of institutional experiences must 
    be measured \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.

    \item \textbf{Environment} encompasses the full range of institutional 
    experiences, programmes, policies, and interactions to which the student 
    is exposed during enrollment. This includes both tangible elements 
    (classrooms, library resources, residential halls) and intangible 
    elements (teaching pedagogy, administrative policies, peer group 
    composition, and the quality of faculty interaction). Environmental 
    variables are particularly significant in the I-E-O framework because 
    they represent the factors that educators and institutional administrators 
    can directly control to maximize talent development 
    \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. Applied research using this framework has 
    confirmed that student-advisor interaction, student-faculty interaction, 
    extracurricular participation, and library utilization are each positively 
    and independently associated with first-to-second year retention 
    \parencite{Xiao2020}.

    \item \textbf{Outputs} (or Outcomes) represent the measurable results of 
    the educational programme: academic achievement, retention, degree 
    completion, and the full range of skills, values, and attitudes the 
    institution aims to develop. In the I-E-O framework, outcomes are 
    understood as the product of how environmental conditions transform a 
    student's initial input characteristics. Evaluating outcomes without 
    accounting for inputs is explicitly identified by Astin as an incomplete 
    assessment design, as it fails to capture how much students actually 
    developed relative to their starting point \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Methodological Logic: The Natural Experiment}

A defining methodological feature of the I-E-O model is its use of what 
Astin terms \textit{natural experiments}. Because students cannot be randomly 
assigned to different institutional environments, the model relies on complex 
multivariate statistical analyses—such as multiple logistic regression—to 
control for input differences and isolate the true effect of environmental 
variables on outcomes \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. This approach offers two 
practical advantages. First, it avoids the artificial conditions of laboratory 
experiments by studying real institutional variation. Second, it allows 
researchers to examine the simultaneous effects of many environmental 
variables, which is precisely the analytical challenge confronted in this 
study's multi-stream data pipeline. The machine learning models developed in 
Chapter Three operationalize this same logic: by training on historical student 
cohort data with known outcomes (2021--2024), the models statistically isolate 
which combinations of input and environmental variables most strongly predict 
each risk outcome, mirroring Astin's natural experiment methodology in a 
computational framework.

\subsubsection{Complementarity with Tinto's Model}

The I-E-O model and Tinto's SIM are theoretically complementary rather than 
competing. Tinto explains \textit{why} students depart through the mechanism 
of diminishing integration; Astin provides the \textit{variable architecture}—
the structured categorization of inputs, environmental conditions, and 
outcomes—that a predictive model must measure \parencite{Nicoletti2019, 
Bento2025}. This complementarity is particularly significant for the 
three-outcome prediction framework of this study. Dropout risk maps most 
directly to Tinto's integration failure mechanism. Course failure risk and 
programme delay risk, however, are better understood through Astin's 
environmental lens: they reflect whether the institution's academic policies, 
attendance requirements, and support resources are functioning as effective 
environmental buffers against disengagement. A student may remain enrolled—
not a dropout in Tinto's terms—while still accumulating course failures or 
programme delays that Astin's output variables would capture.

\subsubsection{Operationalization in This Study}

The I-E-O model maps directly and systematically onto the three institutional 
data streams integrated in this study's analytics pipeline:

\textbf{Inputs} are operationalized through Academic Management System (AMS) 
variables: entry qualifications, prior GPA trajectory, school of enrollment, 
and year of study. These correspond to Astin's input variables and establish 
the baseline characteristics against which environmental effects are assessed 
\parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.

\textbf{Environmental variables} are operationalized through LMS engagement 
metrics—login frequency, resource access, and assignment submission 
timeliness—and real-time attendance records. These capture the student's 
degree of active interaction with the institutional environment. Empirical 
research applying the I-E-O framework has demonstrated that student-advisor 
interaction, student-faculty interaction, extracurricular participation, and 
library utilization are each positively associated with first-to-second year 
retention \parencite{Xiao2020}, directly validating the use of engagement 
metrics as environmental proxies in this study. Strathmore's specific 
institutional policies—the 67\% attendance threshold and the 2.0 GPA floor—
function as measurable environmental constraints that the predictive models 
are explicitly calibrated to reflect.

\textbf{Outputs} are the three predicted risk outcomes: dropout risk, course 
failure risk, and programme delay risk. By framing these as distinct output 
categories, this study extends Astin's output framework beyond the traditional 
graduation/dropout binary to capture the graduated spectrum of academic 
difficulty that students actually experience. This extension is methodologically 
consistent with Astin's own recognition that institutional outcomes are 
multidimensional and that talent development can be undermined in forms that 
fall short of full withdrawal \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}.
\subsubsection{Limitations and Critiques of the I-E-O Model}

Despite its widespread adoption, the I-E-O model carries documented limitations 
that must be acknowledged. The model was developed within a Western, residential, 
four-year university context, where full-time on-campus participation is assumed 
to be the primary channel of environmental engagement \parencite{Astin1993}. 
This assumption does not transfer without recalibration to commuter, part-time, 
or non-Western institutional settings \parencite{Karp2008}. 

A further limitation is the model's linear input-to-environment-to-output 
structure, which oversimplifies the recursive nature of student development. 
In practice, outputs such as academic performance continuously reshape 
subsequent environmental engagement, creating feedback loops that a static 
framework cannot capture \parencite{AstinAntonio2012}. These limitations do not 
invalidate the I-E-O model as a theoretical anchor for this study; rather, 
they motivate the adoption of a longitudinal machine learning architecture 
that continuously re-evaluates risk as new engagement and performance data 
arrives, compensating for the model's structural simplifications in a 
real institutional deployment context.

\section{Machine Learning in Educational Data Mining (EDM)}

\subsection{Overview of the Field}

Educational Data Mining (EDM) is a rapidly developing interdisciplinary 
field that investigates techniques for identifying significant patterns in 
large-scale educational datasets to enhance teaching, learning, and 
institutional efficacy \parencite{Bello2025}. Within this domain, Machine 
Learning (ML) has become a foundational tool, shifting EDM from theoretical 
investigation into a practical instrument for data-driven decision-making. 
By applying computational analysis to data originating in educational 
contexts---such as student information systems, Learning Management Systems 
(LMS), and behavioral interaction logs---ML algorithms identify hidden 
patterns to forecast future student performance and behavior 
\parencite{Dirin2022}. A recent systematic review of 72 peer-reviewed 
studies concluded that research in this domain has matured significantly, 
with ensemble methods, deep learning, and explainable AI now representing 
the three dominant technical paradigms \parencite{Aldowah2026}.

\subsection{Core Applications in Higher Education}

The primary applications of machine learning in EDM converge on four 
objectives that are directly relevant to this study:

\textbf{Performance Prediction} involves forecasting academic outcomes---
such as final course grades, semester GPA, or examination results---by 
analyzing historical academic records, engagement trajectories, and 
behavioral patterns. These models allow institutions to move beyond 
end-of-term assessment toward continuous, data-informed monitoring of 
student progress \parencite{Kwakye2025}.

\textbf{Risk Identification} applies ML models to flag at-risk students 
early in a term, enabling timely and targeted interventions before failure 
becomes irreversible. This is the primary objective of the Early Warning 
System (EWS) architecture developed in this study \parencite{Bello2025}.

\textbf{Study Path and Programme Guidance} uses algorithmic predictions 
of student preferences, strengths, and motivational orientations to 
recommend appropriate academic specializations. Research at Haaga-Helia 
University demonstrated that Random Forest (94\% accuracy) and Decision 
Tree (93\% accuracy) are effective classifiers for study path selection, 
outperforming logistic regression when non-linear motivational and 
demographic features are included \parencite{Dirin2022}.

\textbf{Adaptive and Personalized Learning} supports the creation of 
intelligent learning environments that tailor content, pacing, and 
feedback to individual student profiles based on continuous engagement 
monitoring \parencite{Kwakye2025}.

\subsection{Machine Learning Algorithms and Techniques}

Supervised learning is the most widely adopted paradigm in EDM, 
primarily applied to classification tasks---predicting categorical 
outcomes such as pass/fail, dropout/persist, or risk level---and 
regression tasks that produce continuous scores \parencite{Bello2025, 
Dirin2022}.

\textbf{Ensemble Methods} such as Random Forest, XGBoost, and Gradient 
Boosting are consistently identified as top performers in EDM contexts 
due to their ability to manage complex, non-linear relationships in 
educational data more effectively than baseline linear models. Bello 
et al. (2025) demonstrated this directly: on a dataset of 1,163 student 
records, a baseline Linear Regression model achieved an R\textsuperscript{2} 
of only .32, while Random Forest achieved .69 and XGBoost achieved .70, 
confirming the superior generalization capacity of ensemble methods 
\parencite{Bello2025}.

\textbf{Deep Learning} models---including Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP), 
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) 
networks---are increasingly utilized to capture intricate temporal 
interactions between features. While these architectures often achieve 
high predictive accuracy, they operate as ``black boxes,'' providing 
limited insight into the reasoning behind individual predictions, which 
significantly limits their institutional adoption in advisory contexts 
\parencite{Kwakye2025, Aldowah2026}.

\textbf{Traditional Classifiers} including Decision Trees, Support 
Vector Machines (SVM), Naïve Bayes, and Logistic Regression remain 
widely used for their relative simplicity, computational efficiency, 
and inherent interpretability. Their performance, however, is generally 
outpaced by ensemble methods when the underlying data contains 
non-linear feature interactions \parencite{Bello2025}.

\subsection{Data Sources and Feature Engineering}

ML models in EDM rely on diverse institutional data sources, including 
Academic Management Systems, Learning Management Systems, and clickstream logs from digital 
platforms \parencite{Kwakye2025}. Feature engineering is a critical 
intermediate step, transforming raw institutional records into structured 
model inputs such as attendance rates, prior grade trajectories, 
assignment submission timeliness, and temporal engagement patterns.

Research has established that \textit{time-based features}---such as 
the intervals between LMS interactions, the recency of last login, and 
the ``burstiness'' of activity relative to assignment deadlines---carry 
significant predictive power, often capturing early disengagement signals 
that static features such as a single GPA value cannot detect 
\parencite{Dirin2022}. To ensure model stability and address the class 
imbalance that characterizes most student risk datasets---where at-risk 
students constitute a minority---standard pre-processing techniques 
including normalization, one-hot encoding, and the Synthetic Minority 
Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) are routinely applied 
\parencite{Bello2025, Aldowah2026}.

\subsection{Explainability, Fairness, and Ethical Considerations}

As ML becomes embedded in institutional decision-making, three 
interrelated concerns---fairness, accountability, and transparency---
have emerged as critical prerequisites for responsible deployment 
\parencite{Kwakye2025, Bello2025}.

\textbf{The Black Box Problem and Explainable AI (XAI):} Complex 
ensemble and deep learning models produce predictions without 
human-interpretable reasoning, creating an institutional trust deficit 
that prevents adoption by advisors and administrators who cannot 
interrogate or defend the model's outputs \parencite{Kwakye2025}. 
To address this, researchers now employ XAI techniques---most 
prominently SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), LIME (Local 
Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), and Partial Dependence 
Plots (PDP)---to provide feature-level explanations for individual 
predictions \parencite{Kwakye2025}. A Springer study applying 
CatBoost--SHAP to student academic crisis warning demonstrated that 
explainable predictions provide ``intuitive decision support and 
guidance assistance for education administrators,'' validating SHAP's 
role as a trust-enabling layer in institutional deployments 
\parencite{Zhang2021}.

\textbf{Bias and Fairness:} Predictive models trained on historically 
biased institutional data risk reinforcing systemic inequalities, 
particularly for students from disadvantaged socioeconomic backgrounds. 
Without deliberate fairness auditing, a model may achieve high aggregate 
accuracy while systematically misclassifying vulnerable subpopulations 
\parencite{Bello2025}.

\textbf{Human-in-the-Loop Deployment:} Ethical best practice 
consistently emphasizes that ML models should function as 
decision-\textit{support} tools that complement rather than replace 
human pedagogical judgment \parencite{Kwakye2025}. The advisor remains 
the responsible agent; the model supplies evidence. This principle 
directly informs the role-based dashboard architecture of this study, 
where SHAP outputs are presented as advisor-facing explanations rather 
than autonomous risk verdicts.

\textbf{Data Privacy:} Institutional deployment of student analytics 
systems requires compliance with data protection frameworks---including 
GDPR in European contexts and equivalent national regulations---
mandating robust data anonymization, access controls, and transparent 
data governance policies \parencite{Kwakye2025}.

\subsection{Existing ML-based predictive analytics for at-risk students} 

\subsubsection{Georgia State University: GPS Advising System}

\textbf{Institutional Context and Motivation}

Georgia State University (GSU) is a large public research university in 
Atlanta \parencite{GSU2024}. It enrolls more than 50,000 students---of whom 58\% 
qualify as low-income by federal standards, 60\% are non-white, and 30\% 
are first-generation college students---provides the most extensively 
documented and empirically evaluated implementation of ML-based predictive 
analytics for student retention in higher education 
\parencite{GSU2024, Renick2017}. Prior to 2012, GSU's annual dropout rate 
stood at approximately 5,600 students per year, with adviser-to-student 
ratios as high as 1:1,000. Students---particularly those from minority and 
low-income backgrounds---were failing and withdrawing without ever seeking 
help, because the institution had no mechanism for identifying them before 
failure had already occurred \parencite{NPR2016}.

\textbf{System Architecture and Methodology}

In 2011, GSU entered a partnership with the Education Advisory Board 
(EAB)---a specialist higher education analytics consultancy---to develop 
what would become the GPS (Graduation and Progression Success) Advising 
system \parencite{NPR2016, Hechinger2019}. The methodology proceeded 
in three phases:

\textbf{Phase 1 --- Historical Data Analysis:} EAB analysts processed 
ten years of GSU institutional records, encompassing 2.5 million course 
grades and 140,000 student records, to identify patterns statistically 
associated with student attrition and programme delay 
\parencite{NPR2016, GSUCaseStudy2019}.

\textbf{Phase 2 --- Risk Factor Development:} From this historical 
analysis, a library of more than 800 risk markers was developed. These 
markers span a wide range of behavioral and academic signals: course 
registration anomalies (such as a senior enrolling in a course that does 
not count toward their degree), grade patterns, financial account status, 
LMS engagement changes, and attendance indicators derived from Wi-Fi and 
LMS login monitoring \parencite{GSU2024, EdTech2019}. Risk markers range 
from \textit{repeated historical patterns}---behaviours that have 
historically preceded dropout across thousands of prior students---to 
\textit{specific points of change}, such as a sudden drop in grades or a 
significant reduction in LMS usage \parencite{GSU2024}.

\textbf{Phase 3 --- Operational Deployment:} The GPS system went live in 
August 2012. It operates as a nightly batch process: student grades, 
records, and behavioral indicators are updated every night, and the system 
generates push-notification alerts to the assigned academic adviser 
whenever a student triggers one or more risk markers 
\parencite{GSUCaseStudy2019, NPR2016}. Advisers are expected to initiate 
contact with the flagged student within 48 hours \parencite{EdTech2019}. 
The system is delivered through EAB's proprietary web platform, 
\textit{Navigate}, which presents each student's risk score on a 
color-coded dashboard (green/yellow/red) alongside the specific alert 
triggers \parencite{Signal2019}.


\textbf{Outcomes and Impact}

The results of GPS Advising are among the most cited in the student 
success literature. Since its launch, GSU has recorded the following 
verified outcomes \parencite{GSU2024, GSUCaseStudy2019, NISS2024}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Graduation rates increased by 23 percentage points over the 
    decade following implementation, with four-year graduation rates 
    specifically improving by 7.8 percentage points.
    \item The system generates more than 90,000 adviser-initiated 
    interventions per academic year, compared to approximately 1,000 
    annual adviser meetings before 2012.
    \item Over 250,000 one-on-one meetings between advisers and students 
    have been prompted by GPS alerts since 2012.
    \item Achievement gaps based on race, ethnicity, and income level 
    have been eliminated: Black, Hispanic, first-generation, and 
    low-income students now graduate at rates at or above the overall 
    student body rate---the only national university in the United States 
    to have achieved this outcome.
    \item A Boston Consulting Group analysis confirmed that the programme 
    generates more revenue than it costs, with each one-percentage-point 
    increase in retention generating approximately \$3.18 million in 
    additional tuition revenue annually \parencite{Hechinger2019}.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Limitations and Critical Assessment}

Despite its documented success, the GPS Advising system carries several 
significant limitations that are directly relevant to our study's design 
choices:

\textbf{Proprietary and Non-Transferable Architecture:} The most 
consequential limitation is that GPS Advising is built on EAB's 
proprietary \textit{Navigate} platform, for which GSU paid a heavily 
discounted rate of \$150,000 per year as an early adopter 
\parencite{Hechinger2019}. Market-rate licensing costs are substantially 
higher for most institutions in the Global South, 
including Kenyan universities operating under severe resource constraints. 
This creates a fundamental equity problem: the institutions most in need 
of student success technology are precisely those least able to afford 
commercial vendor solutions.

\textbf{Lack of Model Transparency:} Because the underlying algorithmic 
logic of the \textit{Navigate} platform is proprietary, institutions 
cannot inspect, audit, or modify the predictive models. This opacity 
prevents the kind of institution-specific calibration that this study 
argues is essential: Strathmore University's 67\% attendance threshold 
and 2.0 GPA floor require that predictive models be explicitly tuned 
to local policy parameters, which is impossible within a black-box 
commercial system \parencite{Hechinger2019, Signal2019}.

\textbf{Bias Risk from Historical Data:} The risk markers were derived 
from historical institutional data that may encode pre-existing systemic 
inequalities. Scholars have raised concerns that predictive systems 
trained on historically biased data risk reinforcing those patterns, 
particularly when racial and socioeconomic variables are correlated with 
risk markers \parencite{Hechinger2019}. Although GSU explicitly excludes 
race from its algorithmic inputs, proxy variables such as financial aid 
status and registration patterns may still function as demographic 
proxies \parencite{Hechinger2019}.

\textbf{Resource-Intensive Implementation:} GSU's success was not 
solely the product of technology. More than 90\% of the total programme 
cost was in staffing: GSU hired 42 new academic advisers in the first 
six months, reducing the adviser-to-student ratio from 1:1,000 to 
approximately 1:300 \parencite{NPR2016}. This level of staffing 
investment is not replicable in resource-constrained contexts, and 
it raises the question of whether the technology or the additional 
human capacity was the primary driver of improved outcomes.

\textbf{Relevance to This Study}

The Georgia State case establishes two foundational propositions for 
this research. First, it provides proof of concept at institutional 
scale: predictive analytics, when properly implemented and supported 
by human advisory capacity, can dramatically improve retention and 
eliminate equity gaps \parencite{GSU2024}. Second, and equally 
importantly, its limitations define the design requirements for the 
current study. The Strathmore tool intended to be developed in this thesis is therefore not 
simply inspired by the Georgia State model---it is explicitly designed 
to resolve the transferability and transparency gaps that prevent that 
model from being deployed in a Kenyan institutional context.

\subsubsection{Morocco: A Modular and Explainable Machine Learning Pipeline at Mohammed V University}

\textbf{Institutional Context and Motivation}

While the Georgia State GPS Advising system demonstrates the institutional 
impact achievable through predictive analytics at scale, its proprietary 
architecture and resource requirements limit its transferability to 
institutions in the Global South. A critically important and more recent 
contribution to the EDM literature---directly relevant to the African 
higher education context---is a modular dropout prediction pipeline 
developed at the Mohammadia School of Engineers, Mohammed V University 
in Rabat, Morocco, by Bettahi, Belouadha, and Harroud (2025). This study 
is geographically proximate to the Kenyan context, uses open-source tools, 
and explicitly addresses the governance and explainability requirements 
that the GSU system does not \parencite{Bettahi2025}.

\textbf{Dataset and Institutional Setting}

The study used a longitudinal institutional dataset of 17,883 undergraduate 
records drawn from multiple cohorts at a Moroccan HEI. After cleaning and 
feature curation, the modeling matrix comprised 91 features integrating 
four categories of student data: demographic and programme descriptors; 
academic progression variables (cumulative GPA, credits earned, course 
failures, and passed course counts); engagement and attendance metrics; 
and financial and administrative signals encoded as binary hold flags 
(\texttt{HAS\_*} variables) with paired timestamp durations. The positive 
class---students recorded by the registrar as having discontinued their 
studies---comprised 2,856 students (16.0\%), producing a substantially 
imbalanced classification problem typical of real institutional data 
\parencite{Bettahi2025}.

\textbf{Methodology and Pipeline Architecture}

The pipeline was implemented entirely in \textbf{Python 3.12.0} using 
open-source libraries, specifically \texttt{scikit-learn 1.7.1}, 
\texttt{xgboost 3.0.3}, and \texttt{shap 0.48.0}. This is a significant 
methodological distinction from the proprietary EAB \textit{Navigate} 
platform used by Georgia State: every component of the Moroccan pipeline 
is reproducible, auditable, and transferable without licensing costs 
\parencite{Bettahi2025}.

The six-stage modular architecture proceeded as follows 
\parencite{Bettahi2025}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Ingestion and Cleaning:} Automatic delimiter 
    detection; removal of columns with $>90\%$ missingness; median 
    imputation for numeric fields; label encoding for categorical variables 
    with an explicit \texttt{<MISSING>} token; date normalization to 
    elapsed days; and leakage prevention by dropping direct student 
    identifiers.

    \item \textbf{Feature Engineering:} Transformation of raw institutional 
    records into 91 structured predictors, including temporal features such 
    as hold durations (e.g., \texttt{AP\_END\_DTE} $-$ 
    \texttt{AP\_START\_DTE}) and derived academic momentum indicators.

    \item \textbf{Train/Test Split:} A stratified 80/20 split yielding 
    14,306 training and 3,577 test students, preserving the empirical 
    class ratio in both partitions.

    \item \textbf{Model Benchmarking:} Nine algorithms were evaluated: 
    Logistic Regression (LR), Decision Tree (DT), Random Forest (RF), 
    k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), Gradient 
    Boosting (GB), XGBoost, Na\"{i}ve Bayes (NB), and Multilayer 
    Perceptron (MLP).

    \item \textbf{Explainability Layer (SHAP):} SHAP TreeExplainer 
    applied to all tree-based models on the full 3,577-test-case set; 
    KernelExplainer with $k$-means background ($k=100$) applied to 
    MLP on a 300-sample subset.

    \item \textbf{Reporting and Visualization:} Structured output 
    directories storing confusion matrices, ROC curves, SHAP plots, 
    performance summaries, and reproducibility logs.
\end{enumerate}

Model validation used stratified 5-fold cross-validation with a fixed 
random seed (42). A single operating threshold $\tau$ was selected per 
model by maximizing F1 on cross-validation folds and then fixed for 
all test and subgroup reporting \parencite{Bettahi2025}.

XGBoost achieved the highest performance across all primary metrics: 
AUC-ROC of 0.993, F1-score of 0.911, and recall of 0.944. McNemar's 
paired significance testing confirmed that XGBoost was statistically 
superior to Gradient Boosting ($p = 0.008$) on the test set, while the 
difference from Random Forest was not statistically significant 
($p = 0.428$) \parencite{Bettahi2025}. To assess generalizability, the 
full pipeline was replicated on the public UCI Portugal dataset 
\parencite{Realinho2021}, where XGBoost remained top-ranked (F1-score 
= 0.792, AUC-ROC = 0.922), confirming model ordering preservation across 
feature-shifted datasets.

\textbf{Limitations}

The authors explicitly acknowledge several limitations directly relevant 
to this study's design \parencite{Bettahi2025}:

\textbf{Institution-Specific Features:} Administrative hold timestamps 
such as \texttt{AP\_START\_DTE} and \texttt{AP\_END\_DTE} are 
Moroccan HEI-specific and may not exist or carry equivalent semantics 
in other institutional contexts. This motivates this study's 
institution-specific feature engineering strategy, which maps 
Strathmore's 67\% attendance threshold and 2.0 GPA floor as 
policy-calibrated features rather than relying on transferable generic 
variables.

\textbf{Batch Processing:} The pipeline is designed as a static 
train/test evaluation rather than a real-time streaming system. 
Deployment as a live Early Warning System would require architectural 
extension to support continuous model retraining as new semester data 
arrives.

\textbf{Relevance to This Study}

The Bettahi et al. (2025) pipeline is the most methodologically aligned 
existing system in the literature relative to the platform developed in 
this thesis. Three direct connections are drawn. First, the use of 
Python with open-source libraries (\texttt{scikit-learn}, 
\texttt{xgboost}, \texttt{shap}) establishes the technical feasibility 
of deploying a high-performance, explainable EWS without commercial 
licensing---directly addressing the transferability limitation of the 
Georgia State model. Second, the confirmation of XGBoost as the 
dominant algorithm for structured, imbalanced HEI data on an African 
institutional dataset validates the algorithm selection rationale for 
this study. Third, the SHAP-based risk versus protective factor 
distinction provides the explainability architecture template for the 
role-based advisor dashboards developed in Chapter Four. The key 
departure of this study is then  the simultaneous prediction of three distinct risk outcomes---dropout risk, 
course failure risk, and programme delay risk---rather than a single 
binary dropout outcome, and the explicit calibration of all models to 
Strathmore University's specific policy thresholds.

\subsubsection{South Africa: Machine Learning for Engineering Student 
Success at the University of Johannesburg}

One of the earliest investigations of ML-based student performance 
prediction in the South African context was conducted by Taodzera, Twala, 
and Carroll at the University of Johannesburg's Department of Electrical 
and Electronics Engineering Science \parencite{Taodzera2016}. South 
Africa presents a uniquely complex setting for educational data mining: 
engineering programmes face critically low throughput rates, students 
arrive with profoundly unequal secondary school preparation resulting 
from the enduring effects of apartheid-era education, and socioeconomic 
disparity produces wide variation in academic readiness within the same 
classroom \parencite{Taodzera2016}.

\textbf{Dataset and Features}

The study worked with 1,366 engineering 
student records drawn from a South African university. Available features 
spanned demographic and academic entry indicators: high school 
Mathematics score, Physics score, English score, Life Orientation score, 
ethnicity, home language, age, school province, and religion. The 
response variable was defined as the number of subjects passed to 
qualify for progression to second year---a binary pass/fail 
classification motivated by the institution's own progression 
regulations \parencite{Taodzera2016}.

\textbf{Feature Selection}

Correlation analysis identified Physics 
score (r = 0.2391) and Mathematics score (r = 0.1836) as the strongest 
predictors of first-year success, followed by age (r = 0.1191) and 
ethnicity (r = 0.0808). The significance of ethnicity as a predictor 
directly reflects structural inequalities in South African secondary 
schooling, where historically advantaged schools continue to produce 
students substantially better prepared for the quantitative demands of 
engineering study \parencite{Taodzera2016}. Wrapper-based feature 
selection confirmed Mathematics and Physics as the primary predictors, 
with ethnicity and school province also identified as significant---the 
latter functioning as an indirect proxy for geographic inequality and 
school system quality differentials across provinces.

\textbf{Models and Results} 

Five algorithms were evaluated: Logistic 
Regression (LR), Naïve Bayes (NB), Artificial Neural Network (ANN), 
Support Vector Machine (SVM), and Decision Tree (DT). The DT algorithm 
performed best with 65.86\% accuracy, an F1-score of 0.658, and a Kappa 
statistic of 0.316---classifying it as a fair predictor. SVM ranked 
second (64.63\%), followed by NB (63.41\%) and LR (63.17\%). ANN 
performed weakest (60.24\%). All models were significantly better than 
the prior South African study by Mashiloane (2015), which failed to 
exceed 50\% accuracy on a Computer Science dataset from the University 
of the Witwatersrand \parencite{Taodzera2016}.

\textbf{Critical Assessment}

The Taodzera et al. study makes two 
contributions of direct relevance to this thesis. First, it demonstrates 
that standard ML algorithms can be operationalized in a resource-limited 
African institutional setting using only enrollment-time features 
available at the point of admission---without requiring LMS integration, 
real-time data pipelines, or proprietary vendor platforms. Second, the 
identification of school province and ethnicity as statistically 
significant predictors highlights a structural feature of African 
educational data that does not appear in the North American literature: 
that secondary schooling inequality is itself a measurable risk factor 
that encodes into university-level performance data.

However, the study carries significant limitations. The overall accuracy 
ceiling of 65.86\% reflects the constraints of using enrollment-time 
data only, without any longitudinal academic performance features. The 
absence of SHAP-based or any form of explainability analysis means the 
model cannot be operationalized as a policy-actionable early warning 
system: advisers cannot understand which specific factors triggered a 
risk flag for an individual student. Furthermore, the modest Kappa 
values (all below 0.32) suggest that all five models classify at a 
level only slightly above what chance would produce, and none would 
meet the precision threshold required for institutional deployment 
without substantial risk of false positives that misdirect support 
resources \parencite{Taodzera2016}.


\subsubsection{South Africa: Bayesian Network Classification for Computer
 Science Student Risk Prediction at the University of Cape Town}

At the University of Cape Town's Department of Computer Science, Hassen conducted a study 
applying Bayesian Network classifiers to predict which second-year Computer
  Science students that were at risk of failing CSC2001F \parencite{Hassen2020}. This was a core data structures 
  course widely regarded as the critical attrition point in the UCT Computer Science major.
   The institutional motivation was explicit: while first-year pass rates remained 
   comparatively stable, the transition to senior courses caused a substantial proportion
    of students to either exceed the standard three-year degree duration or abandon the 
    Computer Science major entirely \parencite{Hassen2020}. This pattern of second-year 
    attrition, compounded by South Africa's post-apartheid imperative to produce technically
     skilled graduates from historically disadvantaged backgrounds, provided the normative urgency for the study.

\textbf{Dataset and Features}

The study drew on 728 student records spanning six cohorts from 2013 to 2019. Features were 
selected through iterative consultation with the course convenor and spanned both pre-entry
 and early in-course indicators: Matric Mathematics mark, Matric English mark, National
  Benchmark Test (NBT) scores in Mathematics, Quantitative Literacy and Academic Literacy, 
  first-year Computer Science course final marks (CSC1015F and CSC1016S), the first assignment
   mark for CSC2001F, and the CSC2001F Test 1 mark \parencite{Hassen2020}. The target variable
    was binarised as Pass (final mark above 49) or Fail (final mark below 50). Several candidate 
    features --- including UCTScore and first-year GPA --- were ultimately excluded, the former 
    because its inclusion would have reduced the usable dataset by approximately ten percent due
     to missingness \parencite{Hassen2020}.

\textbf{Methodological Design}

Three Bayesian Network models were developed using the Netica platform. The first was manually
 constructed from expert knowledge gathered in faculty consultations. The second was generated 
 automatically using Netica's Tree Augmented Naïve Bayes (TAN) structure learning algorithm,
  which approximates variable interactions through a tree structure imposed on a naïve Bayesian 
  base \parencite{Hassen2020}. The third was a combined model synthesising insights from both,
   with direct links to the AtRisk target node drawn from whichever predecessor model demonstrated 
   greater sensitivity for each predictor. The gradient descent algorithm was selected for conditional 
   probability table learning in both the TAN and combined models following comparative evaluation 
   of three available learning procedures.

A notable methodological contribution was the adoption of incremental training, wherein each cohort's
 model was trained exclusively on all preceding years' data and evaluated on the subsequent 
 cohort --- replicating the temporal constraints of genuine deployment \parencite{Hassen2020}.
  This approach avoids the data leakage that afflicts many EDM studies that train and test on 
  cross-sectional splits of the same cohort. Additionally, class imbalance --- a persistent
   challenge in student risk datasets where passing students substantially outnumber failing
    students --- was addressed through SMOTE oversampling, and continuous features were discretised 
    into uniform ten-point intervals in accordance with EDM literature recommending discretisation
     for improved Bayesian classifier accuracy \parencite{Hassen2020}.

\textbf{Models and Results}

The three models produced substantially different performance profiles when evaluated on the 
2019 holdout cohort (n=108). The manually constructed model performed poorly, achieving only 
a 67.59\% accuracy rate alongside a recall of 0.33 and a near-negligible F1 score of 0.10, 
indicating that expert intuition alone was insufficient to capture the probabilistic dependencies 
embedded in the data \parencite{Hassen2020}. The TAN model improved substantially on accuracy (84.25\%) 
and recall (0.50), though its precision remained low at 0.17. The combined model produced the most 
instructive outcome: while its accuracy (83.33\%) was marginally below the TAN model's, it achieved 
a perfect recall score of 1.0 --- correctly identifying every at-risk student in the test
 cohort --- alongside an F1 score of 0.40 and a precision of 0.25 \parencite{Hassen2020}. 
 The study's most important finding was the identification of CSC2001F Assignment 1, CSC2001F Test 1,
  CSC1016S final mark and Matric Mathematics as the variables exerting the greatest influence on the 
  AtRisk node, as determined through Netica's variance reduction sensitivity analysis.

\textbf{Critical Assessment}

The Hassen study makes several contributions of direct relevance to this thesis. First, it demonstrates
 that Bayesian Network classifiers can be operationalised effectively within a South African Computer 
 Science institutional context using a modest dataset without requiring large-scale LMS integration or
  commercial predictive analytics platforms. Second, the adoption of incremental cohort-based training
   represents a methodologically sound simulation of real-world deployment that is absent from many 
   comparable EDM studies. Third, the explicit prioritisation of recall over accuracy as the primary
    evaluation criterion is theoretically well-motivated in the student support context: the asymmetric
     cost of missing a genuinely at-risk student substantially outweighs the cost of providing unnecessary 
     support to a student who would have passed regardless \parencite{Hassen2020}.

However, the study carries significant limitations that circumscribe its generalisability. The dataset of 
728 records spread across six cohorts is small by machine learning standards, and the per-cohort test sets 
are consequently limited --- the 2019 evaluation cohort contained only 108 students, of whom just six were 
classified as failing. This severe class imbalance, even after SMOTE augmentation of the training sets, 
means that the combined model's perfect recall of 1.0 must be interpreted cautiously: correctly identifying 
all six failing students in a 108-student cohort is an encouraging result but constitutes insufficient 
evidence of robust generalisability across larger or differently composed populations \parencite{Hassen2020}. 
The precision score of 0.25 further reveals that for every genuine at-risk student correctly flagged, three 
false positives were generated --- an operational burden that could misallocate scarce advising resources and
 undermine institutional confidence in the system.

The study also notably excludes social and demographic features on ethical grounds, explicitly acknowledging
 that school-level quality and apartheid-era structural inequality are likely significant unobserved
  confounders \parencite{Hassen2020}. While this decision is ethically defensible, it means the model 
  cannot surface the structural drivers of differential risk that are most actionable at the policy level.
   Furthermore, the absence of any explainability framework beyond Netica's sensitivity analysis means 
   that, while a course convenor can observe that Assignment 1 is the strongest predictor, they cannot
    generate student-level explanations sufficient to guide individualised advising conversations. 
    

\subsubsection{Ethiopia: Predictive Analytics at Scale with African 
Institutional Data}

A more recent and methodologically stronger study from the East African 
context was conducted by Esmael (2024) at Wollo University and the 
Kombolcha Institute of Technology in Ethiopia \parencite{Esmael2024}. 
Using a dataset of 32,582 students from academic years 2017--2022 
extracted from the institutional student information system, this study 
applied ML algorithms including Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and 
Decision Tree classifiers to predict final academic results from 
demographic and academic entry features: gender, region, entrance 
examination results, number of previous attempts, studied credits, and 
disability status. Python was used for all preprocessing and modeling.

The study found that ensemble methods---particularly gradient 
boosting---outperformed single classifiers on Ethiopian institutional 
data, echoing the pattern observed in both the Moroccan Bettahi et al. 
(2025) pipeline and the global EDM literature. Entrance examination 
results and studied credits emerged as the strongest predictors, 
consistent with findings from both the South African and Moroccan 
contexts that prior academic achievement and engagement intensity are 
the most transferable cross-institutional risk signals 
\parencite{Esmael2024}.

This study's principal contribution to the present literature review 
is confirmation that ensemble tree-based methods retain their predictive 
advantage over classical algorithms even on African institutional data 
with limited feature dimensionality. Its limitation is the absence of 
any explainability layer (no SHAP or LIME analysis is conducted), 
meaning that model outputs cannot be directly translated into 
student-specific intervention recommendations \parencite{Esmael2024}.

\subsubsection{Kenya: Business Intelligence and Decision Tree Classification for Student Retention Prediction ---case of Machakos University College}

One of the few published East African studies applying machine learning techniques to student retention prediction
 in a higher education context was conducted by Ngemu, Odongo, Omulo, and Manderick, drawing on data from Machakos 
 University College in Kenya \parencite{Ngemu2015}. The study was situated within a broader business intelligence (BI) 
 framing, positioning educational data mining not merely as an academic exercise but as a strategic institutional tool
  for evidence-based decision making in student management. The motivating concern was the well-documented but
   institutionally underaddressed problem of student withdrawal in Kenyan higher learning institutions, where 
   personal, financial, and socioeconomic factors converge to produce attrition patterns that existing student 
   information systems lacked the analytical capacity to anticipate or explain \parencite{Ngemu2015}.

\textbf{Dataset and Features}

The study drew on secondary survey data sourced from Machakos University College's institutional database, 
comprising 270 instances and 14 attributes. The feature set was notable for its emphasis on socioeconomic
 and demographic indicators rather than prior academic performance: variables included course enrolled, KCSE 
 grade, gender, father's and mother's educational qualifications, difficulty in fees payment, parental occupation,
  marital status of parents, sponsor or guardian type, age on entry, whether course expectations were met, health 
  status, and course-programme match \parencite{Ngemu2015}. The binary target variable distinguished between students
   classified as PERSIST or DROPOUT. The deliberate inclusion of family financial circumstances, parental employment
    status, and student health as primary predictors reflects the Kenyan institutional context, where economic precarity
     and household-level resource constraints constitute the dominant structural drivers of non-completion rather than 
     the academic preparation deficits more characteristic of South African engineering attrition \parencite{Ngemu2015}.

\textbf{Feature Selection}

Attribute importance analysis was conducted using both information gain and gain ratio measures within the WEKA 
environment, with Ranker search used to order predictors by significance. Both evaluators converged on the same top 
four predictors: difficulty in fees payment (DFP), age on entry (AOE), parental occupation (PO), and student health 
status (HTH) \parencite{Ngemu2015}. The dominance of fees payment difficulty as the single most informative
 predictor --- with a gain ratio of 0.424, substantially exceeding the second-ranked predictor at 0.153 --- is a finding 
 of considerable structural significance. It positions financial exclusion, rather than academic underpreparation,
  as the primary mechanism of student attrition in this Kenyan institutional setting, a pattern consistent with 
  broader literature on non-completion in sub-Saharan African higher education where fee-dependent public universities 
  serve students from predominantly low-income households \parencite{Ngemu2015}. Gender and course type ranked lowest
   among predictors in both evaluators, suggesting that the withdrawal risk in this population was predominantly
    socioeconomic rather than programme-specific or gender-differentiated.

\textbf{Models and Results}

Four classification algorithms were evaluated using WEKA: the J48 decision tree (an implementation of C4.5), 
Naïve Bayes, Multilayer Perceptron (ANN), and Support Vector Machine (SMO). All four were assessed on ten-fold
 cross-validation accuracy and recall, as well as model building execution time \parencite{Ngemu2015}. The J48
  decision tree outperformed all competitors, achieving a cross-validation accuracy of 94.8\% and a recall of 94.4\%,
   while also being the fastest to train at 0.02 seconds. Multilayer Perceptron ranked second in accuracy (93.4\%) but 
   required more than two seconds to train --- over 100 times slower than J48. Naïve Bayes and SVM both achieved approximately 
   90\% accuracy and recall. On the training set, J48 correctly classified 153 of 162 instances with an accuracy of 94.4\% and 
   a Kappa statistic of 0.872. On the holdout test set, J48 classified 78 of 81 instances correctly, yielding a test accuracy 
   of 96.3\% and a Kappa of 0.925 \parencite{Ngemu2015}. The decision tree structure surfaced interpretable classification 
   rules, including the finding that fee payment difficulty alone was sufficient to classify a student as DROPOUT, while a
    student with no fee difficulties and good health was classified as PERSIST regardless of other attributes \parencite{Ngemu2015}.

\textbf{Critical Assessment}

The Ngemu et al. study makes several contributions relevant to this thesis and to the broader East African educational 
data mining literature. First, it represents one of the earliest published attempts to apply supervised classification 
methods to student retention prediction within a Kenyan public university, demonstrating that WEKA-based BI tools can 
be operationalised using institutional administrative data without requiring specialist data infrastructure or proprietary
 analytics platforms \parencite{Ngemu2015}. Second, the feature set's emphasis on socioeconomic and household-level variables
  rather than academic performance metrics is theoretically well-grounded in Tinto's model of student departure and its 
  African adaptations, and produces a predictor profile that is meaningfully distinct from both the South African engineering 
  context of Taodzera et al. and the Computer Science course performance context of Hassen. Third, the decision tree output
   generates directly interpretable classification rules that an institutional registrar or student affairs officer could 
   apply without specialist data science literacy, addressing a practical barrier to deployment that more opaque models 
   such as ANNs and SVMs do not overcome.

However, the study carries critical methodological limitations that substantially circumscribe the interpretability and
 generalisability of its reported results. The most significant concern is the extreme smallness of the dataset: 270 
 instances with 14 attributes is a very limited sample for constructing robust machine learning models, and the ten-fold 
 cross-validation accuracy of 94.8\% and test set accuracy of 96.3\% are almost certainly inflated by overfitting to a 
 dataset too small to capture genuine population-level variation in attrition patterns \parencite{Ngemu2015}. The Kappa 
 statistic of 0.872 on the training set and 0.925 on the test set suggest strong agreement, but these figures must be
  treated with caution given the dataset size. The study does not report precision and recall separately for each
   class --- a critical omission when the class distribution between PERSIST and DROPOUT students is unknown and 
   potentially imbalanced, since high overall accuracy can mask poor identification of the minority dropout class 
   in exactly the manner the authors of subsequent studies such as Hassen explicitly sought to avoid \parencite{Ngemu2015}.

Furthermore, the study provides no discussion of class imbalance, no oversampling or resampling adjustment for minority
 class representation, and no SHAP-based or equivalent explainability analysis beyond the tree structure itself. The 
 absence of any longitudinal or cohort-based evaluation means the reported accuracy figures reflect model performance
  on a single static cross-section of institutional data, rather than the temporally realistic scenario in which a model
   trained on prior cohorts is used to flag at-risk students in a new intake. The feature set, while theoretically motivated, 
   relies entirely on enrollment-time socioeconomic and demographic data and includes no in-course academic performance 
   indicators --- meaning the model cannot be updated or refined as early course performance data becomes available, 
   limiting its utility as a dynamic early warning system. Finally, the authors acknowledge that social and cultural
    factors could not be collected, and do not discuss the ethical implications of using parental occupation, family 
    financial status, and health as institutional risk flags, dimensions of the deployment context that are increasingly
     recognised as requiring explicit equity and bias auditing before any model is operationalised for advising or support
      allocation decisions \parencite{Ngemu2015}.

\subsubsection{Conceptual Framework}

\subsubsection*{Framework Explanation}

The solution architecture presented in Figure~\ref{fig:solution_framework} 
describes a nine-stage end-to-end pipeline through which raw institutional 
data is transformed into actionable, explainable risk predictions for 
Strathmore University's academic support staff. The framework flows 
strictly from top to bottom, with each stage building on the output of 
the preceding one.

\textbf{Stage 1 — Data Sources:} The pipeline originates from three 
institutional systems operating in parallel: the Academic Management 
System (AMS), which supplies enrollment records, GPA trajectories, and 
academic standing data; the Learning Management System (LMS), which 
contributes behavioral engagement signals including login frequency, 
resource access, and assignment submission patterns; and the Attendance 
Tracking Platform, which provides real-time attendance rates and flags 
students approaching the mandatory 67\% examination eligibility 
threshold.

\textbf{Stage 2 — ETL Pipeline:} Raw data extracted from the three 
source systems is passed through an Extract-Transform-Load (ETL) 
interface that merges records across systems, resolves inconsistencies, 
and validates data integrity before any modelling takes place.

\textbf{Stage 3 — Feature Engineering:} The cleaned dataset is 
transformed into a structured feature matrix. This stage applies 
normalization, categorical encoding, SMOTE oversampling to address 
class imbalance, and the construction of derived indicators such as 
attendance breach flags, GPA floor proximity scores, and temporal 
engagement patterns.

\textbf{Stage 4 — Model Training:} Three ensemble algorithms — Random 
Forest, XGBoost, and Gradient Boosting — are trained on the engineered 
feature matrix using historical student cohort data from 2021 to 2024. 
Stratified cross-validation and hyperparameter tuning are applied to 
maximise generalisation performance.

\textbf{Stage 5 — Prediction Engine:} The trained models generate three 
simultaneous risk scores for each currently enrolled student: dropout 
risk, course failure risk, and programme delay risk. This multi-outcome 
architecture reflects the graduated spectrum of academic difficulty that 
students experience, extending beyond the binary dropout classification 
common in prior literature.

\textbf{Stage 6 — SHAP Explainability:} Risk scores alone are 
insufficient for institutional deployment. This stage applies SHAP 
(SHapley Additive exPlanations) to decompose each prediction into its 
contributing feature drivers, identifying both risk factors and 
protective factors at the individual student level. This transforms 
opaque model outputs into human-interpretable explanations that 
advisors can act on.

\textbf{Stage 7 — REST API:} A role-gated REST API serves as the 
interface between the ML pipeline and the frontend dashboards, ensuring 
that each user role receives only the data and predictions appropriate 
to their institutional responsibilities.

\textbf{Stage 8 — Role-Based Dashboards:} Predictions and SHAP 
explanations are surfaced through three distinct dashboards calibrated 
to different institutional roles. The University Administrator dashboard 
provides a university-wide retention overview and escalation controls. 
The School Administrator dashboard enables course-level drill-down and 
mentor assignment. The Mentor and Advisor dashboard presents individual 
student risk cards with SHAP-driven explanations and intervention 
logging capability. All three dashboards are connected to a push 
notification and email alert engine that triggers outreach when risk 
thresholds are breached.

\textbf{Stage 9 — Institutional Actors:} The pipeline terminates with 
the three human actors who consume the system's outputs: the University 
Administrator, the School Administrator, and the Academic Mentor or 
Advisor. This human-in-the-loop design ensures that the platform 
functions as a decision-support tool that augments rather than replaces 
professional academic judgment.

Taken together, the framework operationalizes the transition from 
Strathmore University's current reactive support paradigm — where 
students are identified only after breaching academic thresholds — to a 
proactive, evidence-driven intervention model in which at-risk students 
are flagged weeks before failure occurs, and advisors are equipped with 
the specific reasons behind each risk classification.
